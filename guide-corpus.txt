===== The Systems You'll Install =====
 The Systems You'll Install A plain-language tour of the whole-home water systems you'll be putting in — the parts, how water moves through them, and how the system types differ. Read with your first job in mind. Almost every first job is the Standard city system — read that part closely; skim the well, H₂O₂, and salt-free types until a work order names one. 📹 Video: Overview of the full system lineup and how each type fits a home — watch it here What a whole-home system actually is Two separate things go into most homes, and they do different jobs. The whole-home unit sits at the main water line where water enters the house. It softens and filters everything going to every tap, shower, and appliance. The under-sink RO is a small separate system at the kitchen sink. It polishes the drinking and cooking water further. It's its own install — see RO Install . Softener vs. filter — not the same thing A softener removes hardness minerals (calcium and magnesium) and heavy metals through ion exchange with salt. A filter (the carbon in the tank, or the RO at the sink) removes chemicals, chlorine, contaminants, and odors. Our standard whole-home unit does both in one tank: one media tank, mixed bed, two jobs. The main parts The control valve (ProValve) The brain on top of the media tank — a ceramic-disc valve. Demand regeneration: you program the home's hardness, and the valve counts gallons and regenerates based on actual usage — not a fixed timer. A max-days setting backstops low usage, so in practice it regenerates roughly monthly, at 2 a.m. 72-hour memory backup — a power blip won't wipe your programming. Bypass included with every unit — it comes with the valve. Never order bypasses separately. Programming is covered in Valve Programming . Here, just know it sits on top of the tank and controls flow and regeneration. The media tank The fiberglass tank that holds all the filtering and softening media. NSF-certified fiberglass. Standard builds ship on a 10"×54" or 12"×52" tank depending on configuration — check the work order . (The unit is still commonly called a "10×54.") The media inside the tank Three materials, layered — order matters. Tanks arrive pre-loaded from the factory; you never load media on site. Carbon (coconut-shell activated carbon) sits on top. Water hits it first — it removes chlorine, chloramine, and chemicals, and protects the resin below. Activated grade on city water; catalytic grade on wells. Softening resin (high-grade resin) sits below the carbon — the ion-exchange resin that removes hardness. Gravel sits at the bottom — it supports the media bed and anchors the distributor tube. The gravel underbedding is included, and like every wetted component — valve, tank, carbon, resin, and gravel — it's NSF third-party certified. On a single-tank city build, the mix is a half cubic foot of carbon over a full cubic foot of resin. That ratio is deliberate: on city water both medias wear out at roughly the same time, so nothing gets replaced early. Why carbon goes above resin: chlorine breaks resin down fast. The carbon strips chlorine before the water ever touches the resin. The distributor tube The center tube that runs down the middle of the media tank. The bottom looks like a pine cone with tiny holes, anchored by the gravel. It's a 1.25" tube — part of how the system holds ~28 GPM with no pressure loss. Competitors run a 3/4" riser; the wider 1-1/4" tube is why flow rate isn't sacrificed. This is the return path: treated water comes back up through it to head into the house. The brine (salt) tank A separate tank that holds the salt, sitting next to the media tank — connected to the valve, but not the same vessel. The resin softens water by swapping hardness ions for sodium. When the resin is used up, the valve pulls brine from this tank to recharge it. That's regeneration. Dimensions vary by build — confirm with the office if you need to verify a delivery. The under-sink RO (separate system) The drinking-water system at the kitchen sink. Its own install ( RO Install ), not part of the tank. Two kinds: the tankless RO (HW800, 7-stage, compact, on-demand — needs electricity ) and the tank RO (5-stage, small storage tank — works without electricity). Alkaline remineralizer: tankless only. The tankless RO has it built in. The tank RO does not — it takes a separate external alkaline filter, which is leak-prone; that's why we prefer the built-in. Tankless RO waste ratio: 2:1 . Add-ons you'll also see Two add-ons show up on work orders — this is why the Before the Job kit list includes brass fittings. UV purifier (wells): a stainless reactor on the main line that disinfects bacteria and viruses with UV light — no change to taste or odor. 120V (needs an outlet), 1" MNPT inlet/outlet. Pre-system sediment filter (optional add-on): the carbon tank already acts as a natural pre-sediment filter for normal grit, so this is an add-on for homes with heavy sediment — a 5-micron, 20"×4.5" cartridge at the main entry that catches sand, silt, and rust before the tanks. Common on wells and older infrastructure; cartridge swaps roughly every 6 months. 🖼 Diagram: Labeled cross-section of the whole-home unit — valve on top, media tank with carbon over resin over gravel, distributor tube down the center, brine tank beside it, and the separate under-sink RO, with arrows showing the flow path — coming soon How the water flows through it Follow the water and the whole thing makes sense. In: water enters the top of the media tank through the valve. Down through carbon: chlorine and chemicals come out first, protecting the resin below. Down through resin: ion exchange pulls out the hardness. Through the gravel and up the center tube: water reaches the bottom, passes through the gravel, and travels back up the distributor tube. Out to the house: soft, filtered water heads to every tap. Drinking water: the kitchen tap gets a final polish at the separate under-sink RO. Short version: in the top, down through carbon then resin, up the center tube, out to the house — drinking water finished at the RO. The system types you'll encounter Most installs are the standard city setup, but you'll see all of these. Standard whole-home + RO (city) The most common install by far. One media tank (softener + filter combined), a brine tank, and the under-sink tankless RO for drinking water. Rated 48,000 grains , roughly 28 GPM . The capacity you program at the valve is a lower regen setpoint — use Valve Programming 's settings table, never the rated number. Fits a standard city home — 1–6 people and 3 bathrooms or fewer (at 8+ residents or 4+ bathrooms, step up to the XL or a dual-tank). 📹 Video: Deep dive on the standard whole-home unit and how it's built — watch it here XL The standard, sized up. Rated 64,000 grains , on a 12"×52" media tank. For larger homes — the standard unit covers 1–6 people , so step up to the XL (or a dual-tank) at 8+ residents or 4+ bathrooms (sqft is secondary). Below that size, the standard unit is fine — the wider distributor tube keeps flow up, so there's no flow decrease staying on it. Dual-Tank City A premium two-tank version for city water. Carbon and softening resin live in separate tanks — the carbon tank is a 10"×60" . Both tanks self-clean (backwash) off one smart valve — the carbon tank uses a simple pass-through / in-and-out valve , not a second expensive electronic one. Most competitors put a valve only on the resin tank and leave the carbon as a passive "passenger" that gets swapped every 2–3 years; because ours backwashes the carbon too, that tank lasts 10–20+ years. Rated 96,000 grains total (48k resin + 48k carbon). The resin never sees chlorinated water, so both medias last much longer than in a single tank. Expect roughly 12–20 years from a dual-tank, and 10–20+ years from its carbon tank — versus ~7–12 years from a single-tank build. Well / Iron & Sulfur Well water needs different media — and above 7 ppm iron, a different system entirely. Dual-Tank Well: for wells. Adds Katalox Light media (removes iron, boosts pH, self-cleans) and catalytic coconut carbon, running an air injection valve. Handles iron up to 7 ppm . Iron & Sulfur (H₂O₂): for wells with iron over 7 ppm or a sulfur / rotten-egg smell. Adds a hydrogen-peroxide injection panel and holding tank ahead of the tanks — the peroxide rusts the iron so the carbon can filter it out, and off-gasses the sulfur. Runs dual valves that stagger regeneration so one brine tank isn't asked to recharge both at once. Salt-Free A conditioner, not a softener — important distinction. No brine tank, no salt, no drain, no electronic control valve, no electricity. (The box still ships a simple in/out valve, tail pieces, and a bypass — that's the whole head, per the manufacturer build sheets.) It conditions the water to prevent scale but does not remove hardness — 10 GPG in is 10 GPG out. It only reduces scale adhesion; it does not actually soften the water or give the silky feel a real softener does. The media is TAC/NAC . You cannot mix carbon into the same tank — that makes the media fail — so a salt-free job needs a separate carbon tank (~$300 dealer) for chlorine/chemical removal. Not recommended for well water. Key numbers to remember Spec Value Media tank 12"×52" (Standard WH+RO and XL) · 10"×54" (No-RO and dual-tank builds) — check the work order Carbon tank (dual-tank builds) 10"×60" Brine tank 15"×35" Distributor tube 1.25" Main water line 1" through the whole path Standard flow rate ~28 GPM Rated capacity 48,000 gr standard · 64,000 gr XL · 96,000 gr dual (48k resin + 48k carbon) — program per Valve Programming 's table, never the rated number RO waste ratio (tankless) 2:1 Pre-sediment filter 5-micron, 20"×4.5" UV purifier 120V, 1" MNPT in/out Well iron cutoff 7 ppm — under it, Dual-Tank Well; over it, Iron & Sulfur (H₂O₂) Related: Before the Job · Valve Programming · RO Install 

===== Before the Job — Prep & What to Bring =====
 Before the Job — Prep & What to Bring Everything that happens before you cut a single pipe: how the equipment reaches you, what to carry, what every site needs, and the install types you'll run into. This section owns the packing list — when any other section mentions parts or tools, this is the list it means. Who provides what We hand you the equipment. You bring the trade skills, your own tools, and the raw install materials to tie it in. We provide (per job): The whole-home softener — control valve, media tank, brine tank. The under-sink RO drinking system — filter bank and faucet. The small parts — brine-tank clip, O-rings, basket, overflow fitting, and the flow restrictor gasket (comes in the white box). One 40 lb bag of softener salt — pour it into the brine tank at startup (first regen), and leave the empty bag at handoff so the customer knows what to buy. RO job kit (packed by the warehouse): the 3/8"-to-1/4" faucet reducer (tank-RO jobs — it's not in the supplier box) and the angle-stop adapter set (3/8"-to-1/4", 3/8"-to-5/16", 3/8"-to-1/2") for the rare sink whose angle stop isn't 3/8". Check they're in your kit at pickup — the job dies at the sink without them. ! The flow restrictor is a must-install, not a spare. The donut-shaped rubber gasket goes on the drain line and limits regeneration waste to 50–60 gallons — skip it and every regen wastes hundreds of gallons. The install step is in The Install SOP 's drain phase. You bring (your tools): Sawzall — cuts the wall opening when there's a board behind the sheetrock. Drywall saw — for a clean sheetrock-only cut. Milwaukee reamer — deburrs cut pipe ends. Crimper — for the fittings. Diamond drill bit — 1/2" or 5/8", either works — for drilling the countertop (granite/quartz) for the RO faucet hole. Straight from the manufacturer's training: "a half-inch or a five-eighths diamond drill bit is what you need for drilling through countertops for the faucet." You supply (install materials): Pipe — including curved "road" pipe for gentle bends off the valve. 3/8" lines. CPVC for the drain run (rated for pressure and hot water). Fittings, 90s, and rings. Drain saddle — for the RO drain tie-in at the P-trap ( RO Install ). It ships in the tankless (HW800) box ; on tank-RO jobs, confirm a saddle is physically in your kit at pickup — whether the tank-RO box includes one is still being verified. Bring a 1/4" drill bit for it (bores the saddle pass-through AND the trap hole). Thread tape (Blue Monster) and pipe dope. PVC glue (primer optional — the pass-through pipes are clean out of the box) — dual-tank jobs glue the pass-through end pieces. Multi-tool — for cutting the drain barb at the first lip before crimping ( The Install SOP ). Brass fittings if the work order includes a UV purifier or pre-sediment filter (see the add-ons in The Systems ). Sizes and quantities: confirm your standard kit with the office on your first pickup — don't guess at diameters or counts. Brass fittings — the per-install count: every install takes two 1" female fittings (one per tail) and one 3/4" female (the drain) — "two tails, one drain." Buy them by the case at a plumbing supply house like Ferguson — roughly $12 a fitting, about $200 a case — instead of one-offs at a big-box store. Most homes you'll tie into are running PEX . Where the small parts ride: the clips, O-rings, basket, and overflow fitting ship inside the brine tank . The kit includes a spare set of the small O-rings — but there is only ONE big O-ring, no spare. Do not lose it. Everything in that tank is required — if any of it is missing, you cannot complete the install, so check it at pickup, not at the customer's house. Test kit: final verification ( Startup & Testing ) needs a hardness drop test, a chlorine test, and a TDS meter. The office supplies your test kit — you don't source your own. It's the standard WET International chlorine + hardness + TDS + iron kit (~$194 all-in with shipping; the standalone iron add-on is the cheap ~$15–20 piece). If you don't have one before your first job, ask the office to send it. Getting the equipment Systems and equipment are picked up at our warehouse — the location is on the work order. If anything ships freight instead: inspect the pallet, photograph all sides, and document any damage BEFORE anyone signs — you or the homeowner. A signed-for damaged tank is an unclaimable damaged tank. The work order also carries the system configuration and the home's tested hardness (GPG). If either is missing, call the office before job day. Verify before you leave the truck Confirm you have every part and every tool before you start — check it yourself, don't assume the warehouse packed it right. Finishing details eat the most time on a job, so keep extras of everything on the truck. A missing clip or fitting kills an install mid-job. The 3 things every install needs A site is installable when all three are accessible. If any one is missing or unreachable, the job changes — or it's not a job yet. Main water line — to tie the system into. Drain — to carry water out during regeneration. Power source — a standard outlet. The unit runs on a standard 110/120V plug — no hardwiring — and the power cord is about 10 feet long. If no outlet is within reach, you have options: splice/extend the cord, run an extension cord, fish the line through the wall behind an existing outlet's face plate, or sub a cheap electrician (~$150) to add an outlet. ! Septic (well/country homes): never route the regen drain into a septic tank — the salty regen water kills the tank's bacteria. Run it outside instead, about 3 ft underground in a flowerbed. If a septic customer asks about drain routing beyond that, call the office — don't freelance local discharge rules. If all three are accessible, it's installable. The install type comes down to where the main line is exposed. Locating the main line and shut-off valve Where the shut-off valve sits determines the install type, so pin it down first — ideally before you're on site. The main is only ever in about three places: Garage / indoor — on the wall, by the softener loop, or behind an access panel. Flowerbed / yard — in an in-ground box. Reading the site — the official install-type tells From the company installation-type guide, the visual tells that set the job type before you cut anything: True Loop — the loop stubs poke exposed out of the garage wall , with an outlet and the access panel nearby: manifold, drain, and power all within reach. The cleanest job there is. Garage Manifold — no exposed stubs; the manifold sits behind the access panel . Water's in the garage, but plan your own drain and power. Front-entry vs. side-entry vs. rear-entry garage — trace the main from the meter at the curb to where the garage faces. Front-entry = the shortest run up the walkway; side- and rear-entry garages mean the main wraps around the house — longer trench, more pipe. Know which one you have before you quote the outdoor work. Cabinet — no workable indoor spot: the system lives outside, above ground, in a weatherproof cabinet on a paver pad . In-ground — trench in the yard, system buried, faux rock over it, valve kept accessible. When in doubt, refer the job for a Site Survey — don't guess an outdoor type from the driveway. ! Fire Suppression Systems (FSS) — never obstruct one Some homes — not just large ones ; some cities require them by area — have a fire-suppression sprinkler system on the household water. Code requires the FSS to be "always on." If the FSS shares the meter with the house supply, the softener must tie in AFTER the FSS split — the softener can never sit between the meter and the sprinklers. The tell: a riser with a pressure gauge and a red flow-switch box (often labeled Potter) behind an access panel — frequently inside a pantry or closet wall. If the home has a manifold, look for the FSS next to it. Find one and you're not sure how the water splits? Stop and refer it for a Site Survey. Softener loop vs. no loop Loop present — a garage loop is the cleanest tie-in. Treat it as the exception, not the default. No loop — most jobs. You're tying into a bare shut-off valve, often in the garage. Plan the tie-in and drain run yourself. Notes on the outdoor types Side entry — trench from the main to the garage. Bury — the valve and fiberglass tank are fine outside with no cover needed; the faux rock protects from the elements. Keep the valve exposed for service. Hard freeze (outdoor units) — tell the customer to drip a faucet until it passes. Outdoor, trenching, and bury installs carry extra fees and need photos collected up front so the job is quoted right. ===== The Install — Step-by-Step (Field SOP) =====
 The Install — Step-by-Step (Field SOP) This is our core install sequence for the whole-home softener: ten phases, run in order, every job. By the end of this section the system is fully plumbed and mounted, the bypass is in BYPASS, and the main water is back on. Programming ( Valve Programming ) and startup ( Startup & Testing ) come after — nothing regenerates in this section. 📹 Video — Full Installation Walkthrough : a complete install, start to finish. Watch this before your first job — it's the single best way to see every phase below done live. Before You Touch a Wrench: The Golden Rule Everything downstream depends on where you tie into the main. Get this wrong and the job fails no matter how clean the rest of your work is. 🥇 GOLDEN RULE — Tie into the main BEFORE the line tees off to the water heater. Tie in before the water-heater tee → you treat both hot and cold . Tie in after the tee → you treat cold only — the water heater keeps getting hard water. One exception outranks the Golden Rule: a fire-suppression system. If the home's FSS shares the meter, the softener must tie in AFTER the FSS split — code requires the sprinklers stay "always on," and the softener can never sit between the meter and them. The tells and the site-survey rule are in Before the Job . Find the tee before you cut anything, and make sure your tie-in sits upstream of it. 🖼 Diagram: the main line entering the house, the tee splitting off to the water heater, and the correct tie-in point marked upstream of that tee — coming soon The Water Sequence — Off, Bypass, Back On The whole section runs on one water sequence. Know it before Phase 1: Stage everything BEFORE the water goes off. Make your cuts on the bench, run the drain, prep every fitting — then shut the main off as late as possible. The customer stays in water while you work; the goal is minutes without water, not an hour. The main goes OFF just before you cut into the live line — you locate the shutoff in Phase 1. Open a faucet and leave it open the whole time the water is off. That's your air escape path. The unit is plumbed in with the bypass valve in BYPASS . Once your tie-in is complete and the joints are checked, the main goes back ON — in bypass, the house gets full city water at full pressure, just unsoftened, so the customer isn't without water while you finish. Push all the air out as pressure returns — like bleeding brakes on a car. Air trapped in the line has to come out somewhere, and if you leave it in there, "somewhere" can be a leak at any joint you plumbed, even inside the wall. Let the open faucet spit and sputter until it runs smooth. Eyeball the tie-in joints as pressure returns — re-checking your own joints is what keeps you off return calls. The system stays in bypass until Startup & Testing startup . The bypass is fully open or fully closed, never in between — it controls pressure, not softness. The one deliberate exception is first-regen startup, where you crack it partway on purpose — see Startup & Testing . Never run a cycle in this section. The first regeneration happens in Startup & Testing , after the valve is programmed in Valve Programming . The Tanks Arrive Ready Every tank is assembled and media-loaded at our factory in Fort Worth . There is no on-site media loading — ever. If a tank arrives looking unloaded or damaged, stop and call the office. No initial water fill. The valve fills the brine tank on its own during the first regeneration — water shows up roughly 30 minutes in ( Startup & Testing ). You just add the bag of salt. Phase 1 — Job Setup & Wall Assessment Set yourself up so nothing stops the job halfway through, and learn the wall before you cut it. Confirm parts and tools first Check that you personally have every part and tool before starting. Don't trust the warehouse pull — verify it yourself. Find the main and read the pipe Locate the main water shutoff , typically behind the access panel — this is what you close before cutting in. Check the pipe size so you know what you're tying into. Plumbing type is usually PEX . If the main is copper or galvanized, call the office before you cut — the tie-in method below assumes PEX. Clear the work area and set up a flat staging surface in the garage. Reach into the wall by hand Before any cutting, reach into the wall cavity by hand and confirm three things: Where the line tees off to the water heater — so you honor the Golden Rule. The wall material — a board behind the sheetrock means you'll need a Sawzall , not just a drywall saw. The plumbing type . Line location, tee location, and wall material — all known before you make a single hole. No surprises mid-cut. Phase 2 — Plan the Drain Decide where the softener drains and how you'll run the pipe before you start fitting anything. Where to tie the drain Tie into the water heater's TPR (temperature/pressure relief) drain — that's our standard. If your local code doesn't allow tying into the TPR discharge, stop and call the office before improvising. Know the pressure difference: the TPR drain is pressurized ; the softener system is not (it runs on its own pressure only). Horizontal vs. vertical tie-in: tie into the horizontal run by default. But the softener pushes its drain water on its own pressure, and on some setups that back-pressure can send water back into the water-heater's drip pan and rust the tank bottom — where that's a risk, tie into the vertical piece instead (water is far harder to push up than sideways). No slope requirement. Because the system pushes the drain water on its own pressure, the drain line does not need the 1/8"–1/4"-per-foot fall a house drain needs — it can run up a stack into the attic, over a door, wherever the room forces it. Route it however the job needs. Use CPVC for the drain run — it's rated for both pressure and hot water. Make it a real, secured connection The regen drain must be a formal, secured plumbing connection — not a loose hose. During regeneration the drain line pushes ~50 gallons/hour with real pressure , and an unsecured hose will blow out. Never shove a drain hose into a vent stack. One installer forced a drain line through an attic vent stack and caulked it — the pressure pushed it right back out and flooded roughly $40k of damage . Fitting philosophy Use the fewest fittings possible — every fitting is a potential leak point. Favor vertical over horizontal . If a fitting ages out, gravity helps you rather than working against you. Horizontal is acceptable, not ideal. 🖼 Diagram: the softener drain routed to the water-heater TPR drain — clean vertical run with minimal fittings vs. a messy horizontal run — coming soon Phase 3 — Assemble the Control Head & Bypass Build up the control head with the small parts that cause guaranteed callbacks if they're missed. The components Bypass valve, control valve, head, and tails. The two parts you cannot skip Seat the bottom O-ring — mandatory. Miss it and it leaks 100% of the time. Install the brine-tank clip that secures the tube — miss it and the line leaks and pops off, which means a callback. Keep the clip and other small parts in a side pocket so they don't get lost on the job. Basket and fittings Install the basket : line up the four grooves until it clicks. Seat every fitting fully — lube if you have it, push down until you hear it pop, then turn it with real muscle. Don't be scared to lean into it. If it comes off a little, pop it back on — and make sure it's tight, because a loose one will leak through. Verify tightness and re-seat anything that feels loose. Phase 4 — Flow Meter & Flow Components Set the flow meter and flow components, and check that nothing is pinched before you move on. Install the flow meter and tighten it down. Inspect every ring and fitting — nothing pinched, popping out, or misaligned. A pinched ring will work its way out and leak . Flow meter orientation: long side IN , and the arrows point IN toward the inlet. If it's unclear which line the flow meter goes on for your build, call the office — don't guess . Phase 5 — Drain Threading & Flow Restrictor Seal the drain threads and install the one part that saves hundreds of gallons per regen. Thread sealing Apply thread tape plus pipe dope to the drain threads. Not strictly required since the drain isn't pressurized — but better safe. Blue Monster tape technique: wrap the threads, then run your fingers over them and twirl. When the lines darken, the tape has caught. The drain fitting's threads are 3/4" — match whatever pipe you're plumbing with to that. The drain barb — cut it before you crimp The drain piece that ships on the valve is barbed, and barbs are for open poly pipe only — you cannot crimp onto a barb. The shop-standard conversion (same on Standard and Dual builds): Unscrew the drain piece (it threads out). Cut at the FIRST (small) lip — not the big lip — with a multi-tool, and pop the whole barbed section off. The remaining piece still seats in the valve; thread your 3/4" female over it — it threads on perfectly. Now you have proper threads you can crimp to , and you plumb the drain from there. Install the flow restrictor — non-negotiable The flow restrictor is the color-coded rubber donut/gasket that ships inside the white valve box — it's not bought separately. It installs on the drain line . Check the drain piece before you install anything: on current valves the restrictor often ships already seated in the end of the drain fitting — unthread the drain piece and look for the "little button" in its end ; that's the restrictor. The drain piece pops out with its clip and threads right back in, so verify it's there rather than assuming you need to add one. It limits regeneration discharge to 50–60 gallons per cycle . Skip it and every regen wastes hundreds of gallons . The correct color for the unit size and how the donut seats are specified in the paper manual inside that white box. Match the color to your unit and follow the manual for orientation — don't guess. Phase 6 — Mount the Wall Plate & Cut the Hole Use the plate's own guide to mark the opening, then cut with the right tool for the wall. Use the wall plate's built-in measuring guide — fold it in and hold it in place. Mark the four corners only . Don't draw a full outline. Cut the opening. Reach for the Sawzall if you felt a board back in Phase 1. Phase 7 — Route the Softener Drain Line Run the softener drain out of the house the same way the water heater drains. Run the softener drain along the wall behind the water heater and out, draining back to the city — just like the heater does. ! Septic home? Never route the regen drain into the septic tank — see the septic rule in Before the Job (run it outside, ~3 ft underground in a flowerbed). Minimize fittings. Finish the pipe end with a 90 + ring every time. You'll need a 90 regardless of which direction you're heading. Phase 8 — Shape & Fit the Pipe Shape the pipe to fit without disturbing the ring that holds everything in place. ! Watch the ring Fittings rubbing against the ring push the clip out . Don't force a fitting against it. Shaping the pipe Use curved "road" pipe — add only a gentle curve, never a hard bend. Ream the cut ends with a Milwaukee reamer for clean, burr-free holes. Phase 9 — Crimp & Mount the Plate Crimp the fittings and screw the plate down so it's solid. Crimping Crimp the fittings, keeping them turned to the side — they rotate, which gives you room for the initial crimp. Pre-position the fittings before you measure the plate dots. Ring sizes and crimp-tool spec for our standard build: confirm with the office before your first job. Mounting the plate Mount the plate with screws — more secure than the clips alone. Skip the bottom two screws — the pipe holds that side. Match everything by eye; this is where experience shows. 🖼 Diagram: the finished wall plate mounted with screws (bottom two omitted), the bypass valve labeled arrow-in / arrow-out, and the crimped fittings turned to the side — coming soon Phase 10 — Brine Tank Assembly Build the brine tank and lock it down. Getting the air out and confirming the connection here is what keeps water off the floor. Bleed and seat Bleed all air before pressurizing — trapped air leads to leaks. The purge method is the open faucet from the water sequence at the top of this section: keep it running while the water is off, and let it push all the air out when pressure returns. Fully seat the brine line — stab it all the way into the push-fitting inside the brine tank. The only brine-tank overflow we've ever had traced back to an unseated brine line. An internal float (like a toilet float) prevents overfilling, so a properly seated line is what keeps the tank from spilling. Overflow and the brine well Install the overflow fitting to handle any overfill. Rubber-side orientation is your preference — just get it tight. Seat the brine well — "the tower." The tower is the white plastic tube that stands vertically inside the brine tank (it houses the float). Plug it firmly straight down into the circular hole in the bottom of the brine tank so it seats and stands vertical — that's what "hold the tower to the ground" means. It is not an anti-tip strap to the floor. Power the valve Plug the valve's power adapter into the wall outlet and leave a little space between the plug and the unit. Don't pin the transformer tight against the tank — leave slack in the cord so it can be unplugged for service. ! Before you ever run a cycle Confirm the brine tank is connected. Finishing touches Leave joints "a little flat" rather than over-cranking. Firm hand-tight is enough. The system clock gets set during valve programming — see Valve Programming . 🖼 Diagram: the finished brine-tank build — overflow fitting, the brine well (tower) seated into the tank floor, and the brine-line connection at the control head — coming soon Dual-Tank Builds — What Changes The phases above are the single-tank flow. A dual-tank city job assembles differently — here's everything that changes. Parts that can kill the install — protect these Two pass-through sticks (the pipes that connect the two tanks) plus their glue-on end pieces ship in the valve box. All of them are proprietary — no hardware store carries them. Lose or ruin one and the install is dead. The valve box also has a baggy: pass-through clips, the brine nut, and two O-rings. Same rule — don't lose it. The two O-rings go in before the heads go on the tanks. Skip them and it leaks, 100% of the time. Assembly order Both tank bottoms are identical — the heads go on the same way. Seat each basket by its ridges: find the sweet spot, push, click — clicked right, it won't move. Tighten each head down by hand. Can't get it tight? Slide a pass-through stick into the head as a lever and turn until it stops. The tanks sit side by side , and a dual-tank plumbs in on the SIDE, not the back — that's how you tell a dual head from a single at a glance. On each head, one port is threaded (that's the bypass side) and one isn't (that's the pass-through side — the two tanks connect to each other there). Pass-through sticks: they arrive in your kit UNCUT — that's deliberate (warehouse policy, confirmed at pack-out). In the training video Honest trims theirs to about 6 inches , but the tolerance is a hard 1/8" — off by that much and the clips will not seat , and you can't buy replacements. Default: use them full length. Only cut if you're completely confident. Glue the end pieces on with normal PVC glue — primer optional (the pipes are clean out of the box; the blue all-in-one glue alone is fine). Bypass assembly The bypass box has its own baggy of clips and O-rings — lose it and you can't build the bypass, period. The arrowed piece fits the long side only — it physically won't seat on the short side, so if it won't go, you're on the wrong port. The two long-side pieces each take an O-ring — forget one and you'll have water everywhere. Hand-tighten, then run a finger around to confirm the O-ring isn't pinched. Clips only pop in when both sides are tightened evenly. If a clip won't go, don't force it (you'll break it) — even out the tightening. The little half-moon piece pops into its slot. Thread tape + pipe dope on the females, then the lines pop right in . Brine and drain The brine nut slides on with the threads facing OUT ; the clip is held by gravity; hand-tight only. The brine elbow seats in the back — push it all the way in or the clip won't pop. The drain fitting is 3/4" threads — and see Phase 5 for the barb-cutting step before you crimp. While you plumb: keep it in bypass Keep the system closed off in bypass the whole time you're plumbing and when the house water first comes back on — otherwise the residue from the fresh media (the "nasty chemical" flush) runs straight into the customer's lines. It only opens at Startup . Where This Leaves You At the end of Phase 10: The softener is fully plumbed and mounted, and the brine tank is built and connected. The bypass is in BYPASS and the main water is back ON — the house has full-pressure, unsoftened city water. No cycle has run and nothing is programmed yet. ===== Valve Programming =====
 Valve Programming The physical install is done: the system is plumbed in, sitting in bypass, and the main water is back on ( The Install SOP ). Now program the control valve (the ProValve) so the softener knows how big it is, how hard the water is, and when to clean itself. This section is programming only — the first regeneration is a startup step, covered in Startup & Testing . 📹 Video: Valve assembly + programming walkthrough, every menu screen on camera — watch it here (public YouTube — no sign-in needed). A second programming-only walkthrough lives on Drive . Before You Start The valve does the work automatically once it's set. Your job here is to enter the right numbers. Three things to know going in: The valve runs demand regeneration — programmed with the home's hardness, it counts gallons and regenerates based on actual usage, not a fixed schedule. The algorithm turns the programmed hardness into a gallon counter and ticks it down as water is used; when the counter hits zero, it regenerates. You set the regeneration time to 2:00 a.m. and a max-days backstop so it self-cleans even in a low-usage home. If the gallon counter hasn't reached zero within the day window, the valve force-regenerates anyway — it has to clean itself at least once a month. Net effect: roughly one regen a month, at 2 a.m., in normal use. The single most important number is water hardness , and it comes off the work order — the office tested the home's water and put the GPG on your paperwork. Get it wrong and you get hard-water breakthrough and a callback. Programming these numbers wrong has caused system failures and tank swaps — take the time to enter them correctly. ===== Under-Sink RO Install =====
 Under-Sink RO Install This section covers the under-sink reverse-osmosis drinking system — the faucet, the unit, the storage, and the leak check — so you install ours the same way every time and never leave a slow leak behind a cabinet. What You're Installing The RO is a separate drinking-water system that lives under the kitchen sink and feeds a dedicated faucet on the counter. It is independent of the whole-home softener — its own supply tap, its own filter bank, its own drain connection. Two versions ship, depending on the job: HW800 AlkaPro tankless RO — the standard on most city bundles. 7-stage, 800 GPD, on-demand (no storage tank), with a built-in alkaline remineralizer and auto-flush. Finished water comes out around 8.5–9.0 pH. Needs electricity. Tank RO — a steel-shell storage tank with a food-grade butyl bladder that pressurizes RO water for steady faucet flow. Works without electricity. No built-in alkaline stage — remineralizing takes a separate external add-on filter. Both strip contaminants down through the membrane; only the tankless unit adds healthy minerals back natively (on a tank RO that's an external add-on filter). The tankless unit filters down to 0.0001µm , and its NSF thin-film RO membrane runs 95–99% rejection on TDS, heavy metals, microplastics, PFAs, and bacteria/viruses. 🖼 Diagram: under-sink RO layout — dedicated faucet up through the counter, the RO unit and (if tank) the storage tank inside the cabinet, the drain connection, and the cold-water supply tie-in with the shutoff T — coming soon Filter Stages The tankless unit is multi-stage internally — that's the "7-stage" on the label. Water runs through a sediment pre-filter, a pre-carbon block, the RO membrane, a post-carbon block, and an alkaline filter (plus a few additional stages), and the alkaline filter is now built in by the manufacturer rather than added on. The "7-stage" count and the "three filters" you see are not a contradiction: only three of those cartridges are user-replaceable , and those are the three that sit behind the removable magnetic faceplate. Water moves through the replaceable three in order: Pre-carbon — the large fridge-style filter; first-stage carbon. RO membrane — the fine membrane that removes everything. Post-carbon with built-in alkaline remineralizer — final polish that adds minerals back. A tank RO has no built-in alkaline stage — it takes an external add-on filter instead, and those external filters are a known leak point. Know which unit you're on before you quote a filter change. Yearly filter change (the pop-out cartridges): turn off the angle-stop where the RO ties into the cold-water line, open the RO faucet to relieve the pressure, then push the cartridge button and the filter pops out. 📹 Video: the three RO filters behind the magnetic faceplate, in flow order — link coming soon Plan the Sink First Before you drill anything, assess the sink and plan the whole layout. Tankless RO: confirm a usable outlet in the sink cabinet before you start. The HW800 has an electric pump and needs a 110V outlet under the kitchen sink . Never show up with a tankless if there's no under-sink outlet — carry a tank-style RO as backup. Watch for the outlet being switched off the garbage-disposal switch — check that it's actually live before you commit. No outlet → call the office before starting — don't drill a countertop for a unit you can't power. Confirm whether the sink already has a spare hole for the drinking faucet. A new dedicated faucet hole often needs to be drilled. Check the countertop material. Granite and quartz are harder to drill — that's what the 1/2"–5/8" diamond bit in your kit ( Before the Job ) is for. Plan the layout for symmetry — the RO unit on one side of the cabinet, the faucet run on the other. Map where the tubing and any wiring will run behind the fixtures so no line ends up under tension. Lines under constant tension leak. Tankless RO — Assembly Out of the Box The tankless unit ships double-boxed with the lines, fittings, angle stop, drain saddle, and a shock breaker (water-hammer arrestor). A few things to get right before anything touches the sink: The back ports. Four ports with pull tabs: clean water, waste water, inlet, and pure water (the faucet port). Leave the CLEAN-water tab plugged. That port puts out filtered water that bypasses the RO membrane — it works without power, but it is not purified . We don't use it. Pull the other tabs (push the gray tab in, pull the plug out). The three 90s. Two are 3/8" (faucet and inlet) and one is 1/4" (drain). Two rules: The blue piece always faces the 90 — it marks the direction of flow. The 90 with the shock breaker goes on the INLET (incoming water), blue on the bottom. Big 90, small 90, shock-breaker 90 — that's the lineup. The angle stop. It ties into the COLD line only — never the hot. There's a black rubber gasket inside — do not lose it. Without it the connection leaks, period. Push it in, wiggle it to seat, and tighten — it gets hard by hand at the end, so grab it with channel locks. It firms up, then eases just as it seats. If the house's angle stop isn't 3/8" (3/8" is what you'll see 90–99% of the time), you need an adapter — 3/8"-to-1/4", 3/8"-to-5/16", or 3/8"-to-1/2" — the adapter set is packed in your RO job kit by the warehouse. Verify it's there at pickup ( Before the Job ). Connections are push-to-pop. Every line seats with a firm push — make sure you feel/hear the pop or it isn't sealed. Faucet line (with its reducer) → pure water . Drain line → waste water . Inlet gasket sits on top of the shock breaker — pop it home. Drill the Faucet Hole Drill the dedicated faucet hole through the countertop. Drill the new hole on the right side of the sink (or mirror it) so the faucet sits symmetric and the unit can live on the opposite side of the cabinet. Technique on granite/quartz: Start the bit at a 45° angle to bore a starting notch — go in straight from the top and the bit wanders all over the stone . Bring the bit upright once the notch bites. Add water while drilling to cool the bit. It'll bore through any countertop — marble, granite, quartz, quartzite — and it won't crack the stone , so don't panic about that. The only realistic downside is a surface scratch (~$200 buff/repolish, rare). Take your time. - Bit size: 1/2" or 5/8" is the standard; 7/8" works in a pinch if that's all the store carries — never go above 7/8" (a 1" hole is almost too big for the faucet base). - Catch the stone dust — never let it wash down the garbage disposal. Catch it by hand and wipe up with wet napkins. - Once the hole is through, the faucet supply line pokes up about ¼" above the countertop so the faucet seats cleanly. - The hole is small — the faucet's cover disc (escutcheon) fully covers it, so there's no visible gap. Mount the Faucet The drinking faucet mounts through the fresh hole and seals from both sides. In the faucet bag: the faucet, two black rubber gaskets, a top base, and a bottom screw — inspect it out of the bag (missing parts in a sealed bag are nearly unheard of). On brushed nickel and chrome finishes, peel the blue protective film off before mounting. Install it with both rubber gaskets — one above the counter, one below. The cover disc is larger than the gasket rings, so there's no leak path around it. Snug the bottom nut against the countertop first; once it's snug, the top tightens itself clockwise — getting it fully tight from below is the hard way. The spout swivels; the base stays fixed. Hand-tight, no wrench. Run the Tubing Route every line behind the fixtures and leave yourself room to service the unit later. Keep all tubing behind the fixtures , never pulled tight. A line under tension is a future leak. Leave slack on every line — the most slack on the faucet line. Never cut the faucet line short. The slack is what lets you pull the whole unit out of the cabinet for a filter change or service. 📹 Video: routing tubing behind fixtures and leaving service slack, most on the faucet line — link coming soon Tie Into the Supply and Add the Shutoff T Tie the RO into the under-sink supply and give the homeowner a way to isolate it. Point out the shutoff T during the Customer Handoff walkthrough — it's the homeowner's first move on any RO leak. Drain Connection The RO's reject water needs a drain path under the sink. The RO drain ties into the P-trap ONLY — never into the dishwasher line. The connection is a drain saddle — the two-piece clamp that bolts around the drain pipe. Same saddle on both RO types; on the tank RO the unit end secures with a thread-on compression piece with a plastic sleeve inside (line through, tighten). The saddle install, step by step: Prep the saddle first: bore out the saddle's own pass-through with a 1/4" drill bit (the drain line is 1/4" tubing) — run the bit in and out gently . There's an O-ring in the front of the saddle: pop it off and the connection WILL leak , so go carefully. When it's bored right, the tubing slips straight through. Drill a 1/4" hole in the P-trap assembly — same 1/4" bit. Placement: the P-trap assembly ONLY — the plastic trap pipe (either the large or small section is fine). The saddle physically won't fit the PVC or any other pipe under the sink; it's made for the trap. Force it anywhere else and it leaks — that one's on you. Wrap the saddle around the pipe over the hole, seat the bolts, and tighten. You'll know it's connected right at startup: when the unit powers on, it primes the lines and you'll see the drain kick as reject water starts moving. The Storage Tank (Tank RO Only) If the job is a tank RO rather than the tankless HW800, the storage tank sits in the cabinet: It's a steel shell with a food-grade butyl bladder that stores and pressurizes RO water for steady flow. It fits under most kitchen sinks and works without electricity. The tankless HW800 has no storage tank at all — it's on-demand. Tank RO — assembly out of the box The tank RO ships partially unassembled. Four things to get right: Tank connector — hand-tight only. The connector is metal threads into a plastic female. Wrap the threads with thread tape only, 3–4 times around — no pipe dope — and tighten by hand until it resists, then stop. Never put a wrench on it: the piece is specific to this tank and isn't sold at Home Depot, Lowe's, or most plumbing stores — break it and the install is dead. Tight, no wiggle, leave it alone. The RO membrane ships OUTSIDE its housing — you install it. It's not a spare filter; without it the unit isn't an RO. Unscrew the membrane housing, slide the membrane in rubber-ring end facing out (bare end goes in first), push it fully home, and re-tighten the housing. IN vs OUT on the top housings: both are labeled on top, and both INs are 3/8". The two OUT fittings differ — the smaller head is the OUT (it takes the 1/4" line); the bigger head goes on IN. The loose factory-attached line connects to the OUT — it carries water up to the top two cartridges: the RO membrane and the taste-and-odor polish filter (that top filter is not an alkaline stage — alkaline on a tank RO is the external add-on). Line labels: the unit's ports read FAUCET / TANK / IN . IN goes to the angle stop , FAUCET to the drinking faucet, TANK to the storage tank. Mnemonic from the shop: the 90 by the OUT goes to the faucet; the TEE is for TANK. Tank RO — connections and tubing Four connections total: inlet (angle stop) · drain (drain saddle) · tank (the tee) · faucet. Everything else runs like the tankless install. Tubing ships as bulk coils of 3/8" and 1/4" line — you cut to fit (unlike the tankless's pre-separated lines). The 3/8"-to-1/4" faucet reducer is NOT in the supplier box — the warehouse packs one in your tank-RO job kit. You need it to join the 3/8" faucet line to the 1/4" faucet stub. Verify it's in your kit at pickup (it's in the Before the Job kit list). Connect the tank LAST. Hook up all the other lines first — depending on the cabinet, the drain plumbing, and the disposal, you may have to slide the tank under something to stand it up on its stand, so leave yourself the room. Tank RO — the startup flush (mandatory) Unlike the tankless (which is drinkable immediately), the tank RO must be flushed before the customer drinks from it — walk the customer through this at handoff: After install, leave the RO faucet closed for ~1.5 hours while the tank fills. Open the RO faucet and drain the tank down to a bare trickle (water never fully stops — a slow trickle means empty), then close it. Let it refill another ~1.5 hours , then drain to a trickle a second time. Close the faucet and let it fill a third time — the third fill is good to drink. Total: about 2–3 hours after installation before the system delivers drinking water. Set that expectation with the customer before you leave. Power On, Flush, and Leak Check Do this with the unit still out in the open — do not tuck anything into the back of the cabinet yet. ! Water ON first — NEVER plug the unit in dry. The system must be full of water before it gets power; plugging in empty can glitch the electronics or trap air in the unit. With all lines connected, no water comes out when you open the supply — and that fill-up window doubles as your first leak check. Only after the lines are full: power the RO on while it's still exposed. Air escaping is a good sign — it means the unit is priming all filters to drain. The system self-primes and flushes: the drain kicks and the water clears. On the tankless , there is no run-in time required before the water is drinkable — it primes electronically. On a tank RO , the water is NOT drinkable yet — run the startup flush above (~2–3 hours, two drain-downs). Watch every fitting for leaks while everything is still visible. A leak you miss here becomes a hidden slow leak the customer finds weeks later. On a tankless unit, expect a roughly quarter-second pressure delay when the RO faucet first opens — the pump spins up on demand. That short dip is normal; mention it at handoff so it doesn't read as a fault. Only after the leak check passes do you tuck the unit and the lines neatly to the back of the cabinet. 📹 Video: powering the RO on while exposed, air/drain kick, and the leak check before tucking — link coming soon Fridge Lines: The Policy We don't tie ROs into refrigerators.  Startup, Testing & Verification Bring the system online, run the mandatory first regeneration, prove everything is dry under real flow, and verify the results with water tests — then run the final checklist before the customer walkthrough. Where You Should Be Don't start this section until all three are true: The Install SOP complete: system fully plumbed and mounted, bypass valve in BYPASS , main water back on, house repressurized, tie-in joints checked dry. Do the steps below in order. Never skip verification — a problem caught now is a five-minute fix instead of a return call. Startup Checks Confirm the clock The unit resumes on re-plug, and a correct clock is what keeps gallons-remaining and the regeneration schedule accurate. Confirm the display clock reads the way Valve Programming 's programming left it. Confirm the regeneration programming The valve runs demand regeneration — programmed with the home's hardness, it counts gallons and regenerates based on actual usage. The max-days setting is a backstop that forces a self-clean even in a low-use home. Net effect: roughly monthly, at 2 a.m., while the customer sleeps. Never set or describe it as a fixed monthly timer. Verify against Valve Programming (programming lives there — this is a check, not a re-teach): Hardness = the tested GPG on the work order . You weren't at the sales test — the office puts the number on the work order. If it's missing, call the office or run the hardness drop test below on untreated water before trusting any number. Regen time = 2:00 a.m. Max-days backstop set per Valve Programming 's table (14–28 days depending on hardness). Confirm brine and drain before any cycle ! Never run a cycle with the brine tank disconnected. Running the system to zero with no brine tank shoots water straight out the open port. Brine line connected, clip in place. Drain line connected and routed out, flow restrictor installed ( The Install SOP ). Bring It Into Service — the First Regeneration Do not put the system into service straight off the truck. The first thing the softener does on your install is a full manual regeneration/backwash . Why it's mandatory The first regen rinses the carbon media's fines and dust out to drain and settles the media in the tank. Skip it and the carbon underperforms from day one because the fines never rinse out. Run it Start the regen — single-tank head: hold down the regen button until the cycle starts (the same forced-regen move Troubleshooting gives customers). Dual head: press regen/back — you'll hear the motor start in backwash. (Backing out of the programming menu with two Back presses also launches one — that's why Valve Programming step 12 says wait until you're here.) The brine tank isn't needed during backwash — but it must be connected before the countdown hits zero , or water shoots out the open port. The first backwash runs about 6 minutes (Standard tank; 10 minutes on XL and Dual ) — experienced installers use that window to finish the brine tank. Bypass technique: before you open the bypass at all, open the outside spigot all the way ( spigot flush below ) so the media color flushes outside, not into the house lines. Then crack the bypass open slowly — halfway during the FIRST backwash , then all the way open during the SECOND backwash . Once fully open it stays fully open — never in between. After the first backwash, skip straight to the second backwash (don't sit through the intermediate cycles). Dual-tank variant: Press regen/back — the motor starts in backwash, the countdown shows. Open the bypass halfway — hold ~30 seconds while you check the head-to-head connections and the drain for leaks. Open it all the way . Let the full regeneration run — about 1.5 to 2 hours. No cycle-skipping on the dual. - Let the cycles run fully to completion, and stay with the unit — this is your leak check under real flow. - Don't pre-fill the brine tank with water. The system fills it on its own — water shows up in the tank roughly 30 minutes in, about when you're wrapping up. Just start it with the one bag of salt. 📹 Video: starting a manual regeneration on the control head, and what a healthy backwash looks and sounds like — link coming soon Leak-check under regeneration flow You already leak-checked the RO in the open ( RO Install ). Now check the softener and every joint you touched while the regen is running and the system is under its own flow. Re-check every joint — no pinched or backed-out rings. A pinched ring works its way out and leaks later — re-seat it now. Never leave a fitting forced against a ring — rubbing pushes the clip out. Confirm brine tank, drain, bypass, and shutoff T are all connected and correctly oriented. Catching a weep now prevents the hidden slow leak the customer finds weeks later. Give the home time to come into soft water Allow roughly 30 minutes for the house to come fully into soft water while the residual hard water in the lines clears — longer in two-story homes. This matters for the hardness test below: the cold supply can read high right after startup simply because old water is still flushing through. Flush the lines: you can never fully drain a house — there's always old water caught in the lines. Post-Install Water Tests Now prove the system delivers. The rule that trips up new installers is what you test where — get it wrong and a working system looks like a failed install. Test kit: hardness vial + Solutions 1 & 2, chlorine vial + reagent, TDS meter. The office supplies your test kit — you don't source your own (the standard WET International chlorine + hardness + TDS + iron kit — see Before the Job ). If you don't have one before your first job, ask the office to send it. The one rule to memorize Whole-home (softened) water: test hardness (chlorine optional). Never test TDS here. RO faucet water: test TDS only. A softener does not lower TDS, so a TDS reading on whole-home water barely moves after install — it looks broken even though it's working perfectly. TDS only drops on water that has passed through the RO. The outside-spigot flush-and-test (the shop method) The cleanest place to flush and test the whole-home water is the outside water hose spigot — it's the one line that branches to nothing else, and it's plumbed right after the shutoff, so treated water hits it first. Make sure nobody inside is running water. Open the outside spigot all the way and let it run. If you followed the regen sequence above, the bypass is already fully open — leave it . (Coming at this cold: open it now.) You'll hear the tanks fill, then the spigot spits air , then the resin and carbon color flushes out. When the water runs clear, test hardness right there at the spigot. Hardness test — cold supply Test the cold supply — the hot side can carry residual hardness from the old water heater. Tools: hardness vial, Solution 1 (indicator), Solution 2 (titrant), cold faucet water. Fill the vial with cold water to the "1 grain per gallon" line . Add 7 drops of Solution 1 , cap, and shake. Add Solution 2 one drop at a time , counting, until the color turns. How to read it: Turns blue immediately, never pink = 0 GPG . That's your pass. If it goes pink first, the number of Solution 2 drops to turn it blue = grains per gallon. You want 0 . Two things so a good install doesn't read as a failure: Old water heaters may briefly show ~2 GPG on the hot side — that's residual, not a bad install. Test the cold supply. A full hardness retest at the customer's faucet is best done about a month out — residual hardness in the supply lines and water heater needs time to flush, or the cold side can still read high right after install. Chlorine test (optional) Optional at post-install, but useful to confirm the carbon is working. Tools: chlorine vial, chlorine reagent, faucet water. Fill the vial to the 10 ml line . Add 5–10 chlorine drops and watch the color develop (yellow → dark yellow → brown; darker = more chlorine). Post-carbon, you want a noticeably lighter result than the pre-install reading. TDS test — RO faucet only This proves the RO drinking system. Test the RO faucet , not the sink faucet. Tools: TDS meter, cup. Fill a cup from the RO faucet . Power on the meter, dip it, and wait for a stable reading . How to read it: RO water should read ~100 ppm or less (how low depends on the source water). The RO retest can be done right after install — no waiting period. Never run TDS on the whole-home softened water — the number barely changes and looks like a failed install. 🖼 Diagram: two cups side by side — high TDS at the sink faucet vs. sub-100 at the RO faucet, with a red X over "don't TDS-test softened water" — coming soon Final Pre-Handoff Verification Checklist Run this before the customer walkthrough. ===== Troubleshooting & Callbacks =====
 Troubleshooting & Callbacks Most callbacks aren't broken systems — they're normal break-in behavior. Learn the field tells, prevent the avoidable callbacks on install day, and know the short list of situations that are genuinely a warranty claim. The mindset: lead with reassurance, not a truck A freshly installed system needs time to clear old water out of the home's pipes, and some first-week behavior (white particles from the RO's remineralization, residual hardness working out) is completely normal. Before you schedule a return trip, work the symptom against the causes below — a short, confident explanation on the phone solves most first-week calls. Find the symptom, then check causes in order. Lead with the reassurance line before rolling a truck. If it's genuinely a fault, see "When it IS a warranty claim." Softener symptoms "The water isn't soft" (just installed) Work these in order — most land on cause 1 or 2. Just installed. The home takes about 30 minutes to come into soft water as residual hard water clears the pipes. Longer in two-story homes. Hot water still a little hard. Normal for a while. An old water heater is full of buildup, and soft water leaches it back out — expect roughly 2 GPG on the hot side, temporarily . Test the cold supply , not the hot: titration drops that turn blue immediately mean 0 GPG, which means the system is working. Out of salt. An empty brine tank means no regeneration, which means hard water. Refill the salt and run a regeneration. Resin does not just die — if it tested good past about 60 days, the failure is a lack of salt, not the equipment. Hardness setting too low. Confirm the valve is programmed to the tested GPG on the work order — the factory default setting (10) under-treats most local water, and a too-low setting lets hardness break through between regenerations. Reprogram if needed ( Valve Programming ). Genuinely dead within weeks. Rare — this is a warranty path. See "When it IS a warranty claim." "It regenerates every night" The schedule got thrown off. A power outage can reset the days-to-regen counter to zero , so it cycles nightly. Reprogram it ( Valve Programming ). An undersized old unit can also over-regenerate — relevant if you're servicing equipment that isn't ours. "The water stopped softening" (tank may be failing) A failing tank shows up as a slight water-pressure dip plus noticeably worse water quality — the resin "goops up" inside. When you see that, put the system on bypass at the valve so the home runs on raw city water for now, then call the office to schedule a tank swap. "There's dirty water in the salt tank" Normal. That's the brine solution sitting in the tank, not the home's drinking water. It's supposed to sit there, and it can look murky. "It makes noise at night" Normal. That's the regeneration and backwash cycle — think of it as a salt-water car wash. The valve regenerates on demand, roughly once a month in normal use, typically around 2 a.m. "Will the drain line freeze?" (Iowa/Illinois winters) No. The drain line only carries water during regeneration — roughly once a month (the valve regenerates on demand) — so it sits empty almost all the time and is never stagnant. On a hard-freeze night with an outdoor install, tell the customer to drip a faucet as a precaution. "The brine tank is overflowing" Usually too much salt — four bags can push water out the overflow. Recommend keeping it around three bags . If salt level is fine, the overflow fitting may simply be doing its job. RO symptoms "The RO stopped making water" Reset it first. Unplug the unit for 10 seconds , then plug it back in. The built-in safety auto-resets. Lights flashing red means a filter is due. Change all filters together — the others are only days behind. Standard service is all three filters every 12 months at the annual visit. No power. A tankless RO needs electricity — check the outlet and the GFCI. (A tank RO still works without power.) "The RO is leaking" Customer stopgap over the phone: have them turn the shutoff T sideways to kill water to the RO only. The rest of the house is unaffected. Then schedule the visit. On the bench, the usual leak points are: lines under tension lines cut too short external add-on alkaline filters (this is why we prefer the built-in remineralization) "The fridge ice/water has been weird since the install" This is why we talk customers out of fridge tie-ins — pressure and communication issues make the outcome roughly a coin flip, and we only do one when the office has booked it. If a fridge was tied in, quirks like this are expected — offer to disconnect it if it keeps misbehaving. Install-day gotchas (prevent the callback) Most callbacks are prevented on the truck, not fixed later. Build these into your routine on every job. Tie in before the water-heater tee. Tie in after it and you only treat the cold water — the water heater keeps getting hard water. Hand-check the wall cavity first to confirm where you are in the run. Never run a regen with the brine tank disconnected — water shoots out of the open port. Power the RO on while it's still out in the open and leak-check it before tucking it back under the sink. Pre-leak checklist (softener) Run this before you call the job done — any one of these missed becomes a callback. Seat the bottom O-ring. Missing it is a 100% leak. Install the brine-tank clip. Missing it lets the line pop off. No pinched rings. Flow restrictor on the drain. Skip it and every regeneration wastes hundreds of gallons. Well and site conditions Well dual-tank: the Katalox tank takes the downflow / air-injection valve . Verify the labeled valve is on the correct tank before you install. Not 100% sure which valve goes on which tank? Call the office — don't guess. Septic homes: never route the regen drain into the septic tank — the salty regen water kills the tank's bacteria. Run it outside, about 3 feet underground in a flowerbed. Galvanized pipes (older Iowa/Illinois homes): soft water leaches metal off galvanized pipe. Flag it to the customer and recommend a repipe where feasible. High incoming pressure (around 120 PSI): the home needs a pressure regulator . It's not our system's issue, but flag it for the customer. Bring the right parts Bring the right faucet color and the right RO type for the job. Showing up with the wrong one is the top cause of return trips. Rotten-egg smell = sulfur (well). Use the H₂O₂ system . First-weeks "is this normal?" calls These are the calls that come in during the first days and weeks. Almost all are normal break-in behavior. If still in doubt, offer a hardness test on the cold supply — drops turn blue immediately means 0 GPG, which means it's working. "The water is coming out yellow since the install" Walk them through the fix: turn on about five faucets and run them simultaneously for roughly 10 minutes straight to clear it. Cause: the new soft water grabs leftover hardness inside the pipes, plus a little new resin/carbon bleeding off into the water at first. What to tell them: "It's not dangerous — it just looks ugly. Run several faucets together for about ten minutes and it'll clear right up." "Will the softener hurt my lawn / sprinklers?" What to tell them: "The system doesn't touch your irrigation or sprinkler system — it only treats the water inside the house plus your hose bibs. That's how about 99% of homes are plumbed, so your lawn gets the same water it always has." "Could the softener discharge corrode my septic?" Acknowledge it's a valid concern, then reassure. On septic homes we don't route the regen drain into the septic tank at all — salty regen water is bad for the tank's bacteria, so the drain runs outside instead. It's a small, infrequent discharge anyway — about 50–60 gallons at around 2 a.m., roughly once a month . If the customer asks to move or re-route the drain line: don't improvise a new location on the spot. Local discharge rules vary — confirm with the office before promising anything. 📹 Video: the two clearing fixes on camera — forcing a regen with the regen button, and the five-faucet ten-minute flush for yellow water — link coming soon When it IS a warranty claim Most calls are not warranty claims. Anything outside that list — no salt, wrong settings, normal break-in behavior — is not a warranty item. Tank swap When a tank has to be replaced, reuse the existing valve and just swap the tank — about a 10-minute job. Always install a fresh tank ; don't vacuum out the old media and refill it. ===== Curated Q&A (office-approved answers) =====
Q: What size drill bit for the RO install?
A: Two bits on an RO job: the faucet hole through the countertop takes a 1/2" or 5/8" diamond bit (7/8" OK in a pinch, never 1" — start at 45° so it doesn't wander, add water to cool). The drain-saddle hole in the P-trap takes a regular 1/4" bit (same bit bores the saddle's pass-through).

Q: How does the RO drain connect to the P-trap?
A: With the drain saddle: bore the saddle's pass-through with a 1/4" bit (careful — there's an O-ring in its front; pop it and it leaks), drill a 1/4" hole in the P-trap assembly, then bolt the saddle around it. It only fits the trap's plastic pipe — never the dishwasher line.

Q: Where is the flow restrictor and how does it install?
A: It's the color-coded rubber donut that ships in the white valve box — and on current valves it's often already seated in the end of the drain fitting (the "little button"). Check the drain piece first. Color per unit size is in the in-box manual. Skip it and every regen wastes hundreds of gallons; with it, 50–60 gal.

Q: What capacity do I program?
A: Standard 36,000 · XL 42,000 · Dual 48,000. That's the resin-only capacity — never program the brochure rating (48k/64k/96k).

Q: What backwash times do I set?
A: Primary: 6 min Standard, 10 min XL and Dual (dual heads come factory-set at 15 — turn it down). Secondary: 3 minutes on every system (factory 0 — set it to 3; it runs after the brine cycle).

Q: What time does it regenerate and how many max days?
A: Regeneration runs at 2:00 a.m., max days 28. It's demand-based: the valve counts gallons from the programmed hardness and regenerates when they hit zero — or at 28 days, whichever comes first. Max days tunes by hardness: above 30 GPG set ~14; below 25 GPG keep 28.

Q: What hardness do I program?
A: The tested GPG from the work order — never leave the factory 10 (that's a Texas habit; Iowa/Illinois water runs 15–40+). No number on the work order? Test on site or call the office. Don't guess.

Q: How much salt does the customer need?
A: About one 40 lb bag every ~3 months. The brine tank holds three bags — recommend three, never four (four can overflow). Any "water softener salt" works; never pool salt, and never let it run empty.

Q: What does the warranty cover?
A: Lifetime — system dry parts, storage/media tanks, ceramic discs. 5 years — control valve, circuit board, filtration media. 1 year — labor. Year 1 all service visits free; after that a $199 trip charge. Freeze damage is never covered.

Q: How much are replacement RO filters?
A: $200 for the set of three, through our office (wrong big-box filters can damage the system). Change all three once a year — lights are blue when healthy, a filter flashing red is due. No tools; the faceplate is magnetic.

Q: How do I program a well valve? What are the well settings?
A: Well valves are set in GALLONS, not grains — don't carry the city numbers over. Capacity 3,000–4,000 gallons (lands a regen about every 7 days), backwash 15 min, air draw 45 min. Everything else ships preset — you just set time and date. And re-compact the media or you get pockets in the bed.

Q: How does the AIO air-draw fitting go together?
A: Locking nut first, then the ferrule inside it, seat it into the port, and it ties directly into the valve. Two fitting options exist — the smaller one works just as well. ⚠️ The check-valve arrow must point OUT (flow direction during regeneration). Backwards = the air draw won't work. Check it before you tighten.

Q: Where do I tie into the main line?
A: Before the line tees off to the water heater — that's the Golden Rule; it treats both hot and cold. One exception outranks it: a fire-suppression system — the softener must tie in after the FSS split.

Q: What if the house has a fire suppression / sprinkler system?
A: Code says the FSS stays always on — the softener ties in AFTER the FSS split, never between the meter and the sprinklers. The tell: a riser with a pressure gauge and a red flow-switch box behind a panel (often in a pantry). Unsure how the water splits? Refer it for a Site Survey.

Q: What is "the tower" in the brine tank?
A: The white plastic brine-well tube: it plugs firmly straight down into the circular hole in the bottom of the brine tank. It is not a strap — nothing gets strapped to the floor.

Q: What are the drain fitting threads / how do I crimp the drain?
A: The drain fitting is 3/4" threads. The piece that ships on the valve is barbed — you can't crimp to a barb: cut at the first (small) lip with a multi-tool, pop the barb off, thread your 3/4" female over what's left, and crimp to that.

Q: Does the drain line need slope? Can it run up?
A: No slope needed — the system pushes drain water on its own pressure, so it can run up into the attic or over a door. But it must be a formal, secured connection (~50 gal/hr with real pressure) — never shoved into a vent stack.

Q: What fittings do I need per install?
A: "Two tails, one drain": two 1" female fittings + one 3/4" female per install (the valve tails are 1" threaded males). Buy by the case at a supply house like Ferguson (~$12 each). Most homes run PEX.

Q: I lost an O-ring — is there a spare?
A: Small O-rings: a spare set ships in the kit. The big O-ring: there is only ONE, no spare — do not lose it. Without it the head leaks 100% of the time.

Q: Can the drain go to a septic system?
A: Never — the salty regen water kills the septic bacteria. Run it outside instead, about 3 ft underground in a flowerbed. Local discharge rules beyond that: call the office.

Q: The house has galvanized or copper pipe — what do I do?
A: Galvanized: avoid installing on it — soft water leaches the metal; recommend a repipe. Copper main: our documented method assumes PEX — call the office before you cut.

Q: Does the tankless RO need power?
A: Yes — a 110V outlet under the sink (it has a pump). Check the outlet isn't dead on the garbage-disposal switch. No outlet = no tankless: carry a tank-style RO as backup. And never plug it in dry — water on first, always.

Q: Which RO ports get used? What's the shock breaker?
A: Four ports: pure (faucet), waste (drain), inlet — and CLEAN WATER STAYS PLUGGED (it bypasses the membrane; filtered, not purified). The 90 with the shock breaker goes on the inlet; the blue piece always faces the 90. Push every line until it pops.

Q: The angle stop isn't 3/8 — what adapter?
A: 3/8" is what you'll see 90–99% of the time. If not: 3/8-to-1/4, 3/8-to-5/16, or 3/8-to-1/2 adapters — not in the supplier box; the warehouse packs the adapter set in your RO job kit — verify at pickup. Cold line only, and don't lose the black gasket inside the angle stop (lose it = leak).

Q: Can I hook the RO to the fridge / ice maker?
A: We talk customers out of it — it goes smoothly about half the time. If the office booked it (+$300–400): prefer a tank-style RO for steadier pressure, route through the attic and drop down, or up from the basement, and warn them about quirks.

Q: How do I run the first regeneration?
A: Kick off the regen, then: bypass halfway during the first backwash → fully open during the second. On a single tank, skip straight from first to second backwash. On a dual: open halfway ~30s, leak-check, open fully, and let the whole cycle run (~1.5–2 h) — no skipping.

Q: How do I test the water after install?
A: Use the outside spigot (the only non-branching line): open it fully, open the bypass, let it spit air and media color, then test when clear — hardness should read 0 GPG. TDS only at the RO faucet, never on softened house water.

Q: Does the RO water need to run before drinking?
A: Tankless: drinkable immediately — it primes itself electronically; no run-in time. Tank RO: NOT immediately — run the mandatory startup flush: fill ~1.5 h, drain to a bare trickle, refill ~1.5 h, drain to a trickle again; the third fill is good to drink (~2–3 h total). Walk the customer through it at handoff.

Q: What power does the softener need?
A: Standard 110/120V plug-in, ~10 ft cord. No outlet nearby? Splice/extend the cord, run an extension, fish it behind an outlet plate, or sub an electrician (~$150).

Q: Where does the softener drain tie in?
A: Into the water heater's TPR drain — horizontal run by default; use the vertical piece where back-pressure could push water into the water-heater pan and rust it. CPVC only (rated for pressure + hot water). Local code says no? Stop and call the office.

Q: What's different about dual-tank assembly?
A: Duals plumb in on the side (not the back). The pass-through sticks and their glue ends are proprietary — no store carries them; don't cut them unless you're sure (off by 1/8" = clips won't seat). O-rings in before the heads go on. Normal PVC glue, primer optional.

Q: How do I get paid / mark a job complete?
A: Send the office 20–30 photos of the finished install + the customer's name. The office marks it complete, and that releases payment.

Q: Who do I call with a question on a job?
A: The office: 760-705-6391 · ryan@passionexteriors.com — 7 days a week. Check this guide first; if the answer's not here, we want to know so we can add it.

Q: What do I tell the customer about freezing?
A: Below freezing: let a faucet drip overnight — small, slow, steady (not full flow). Insulate outdoor lines; a cover over the valve helps. Freeze damage is never covered by warranty, so say it at handoff.

Q: Bypass valve — open, closed, or in between?
A: Fully open or fully closed — never in between (in between only strangles pressure). The one exception: during the first regeneration you crack it halfway, then fully open on the second backwash. Keep it closed while you plumb so media residue never enters the house lines.

Q: What are the tank dimensions?
A: Media tank 12"×52" (Standard WH+RO, XL) or 10"×54" (No-RO, dual builds — check the work order) · brine tank 15" × 35" (square) · dual carbon tank 10" × 60". Brine holds three bags of salt.

Q: Which tank size — when do we upsize to XL?
A: Standard covers 1–6 residents. Upsize to XL at 8+ residents or 4+ bathrooms. Sizing is set by the office on the work order — flag it if the house doesn't match.

Q: How much water does the tank RO hold / how fast does it make water?
A: The tank RO runs a 100 GPD membrane. The storage tank is 4.8 gal (18 L) nominal, which is ~2.5–3 gallons usable — quote the usable number to customers, never the nominal. (The tankless HW800 is 800 GPD, on-demand, no tank.)

Q: What's the number-of-people setting on the dual head?
A: The dual head asks household size — shop default is 4. Set it honestly: raising it lowers the gallons between regens.

Q: What is a True Loop / garage manifold / how do I read the site?
A: True Loop = stubs exposed + outlet + panel (everything in reach). Garage Manifold = manifold behind the access panel. Front/side/rear-entry garage = trace the main from the curb meter (sets your trench). Cabinet and In-Ground are the outdoor options. When in doubt, refer for a Site Survey.

Q: What goes in the brine tank / how does the brine line connect?
A: Seat the brine well ("the tower") into the hole in the tank floor, fit the overflow, and stab the brine line ALL the way into the push fitting — the only overflow we've ever had was an unseated line. No pre-fill: the system fills itself ~30 min in. Pour the 40 lb bag in when you start the first regen, and leave the empty bag for the customer at handoff.

Q: What do I bring to every job?
A: Your tools (sawzall, drywall saw, reamer, crimper, diamond bit, multi-tool, 1/4" bit), pipe + 3/8" lines + CPVC, fittings/90s/rings, thread tape + pipe dope, PVC glue, the brass fittings (two 1" + one 3/4"). The warehouse packs the RO reducer + angle-stop adapters in your job kit — verify at pickup. Full list with sizes in Before the Job.
