The Systems You'll Install

A plain-language tour of the whole-home water systems you'll be putting in — the parts, how water moves through them, and how the system types differ.

Read with your first job in mind. Almost every first job is the Standard city system — read that part closely; skim the well, H₂O₂, and salt-free types until a work order names one.

What a whole-home system actually is

Two separate things go into most homes, and they do different jobs.

  • The whole-home unit sits at the main water line where water enters the house. It softens and filters everything going to every tap, shower, and appliance.
  • The under-sink RO is a small separate system at the kitchen sink. It polishes the drinking and cooking water further. It's its own install — see RO Install.

Softener vs. filter — not the same thing

  • A softener removes hardness minerals (calcium and magnesium) and heavy metals through ion exchange with salt.
  • A filter (the carbon in the tank, or the RO at the sink) removes chemicals, chlorine, contaminants, and odors.

Our standard whole-home unit does both in one tank: one media tank, mixed bed, two jobs.

The main parts

The control valve (ProValve)

The brain on top of the media tank — a ceramic-disc valve.

  • Demand regeneration: you program the home's hardness, and the valve counts gallons and regenerates based on actual usage — not a fixed timer. A max-days setting backstops low usage, so in practice it regenerates roughly monthly, at 2 a.m.
  • 72-hour memory backup — a power blip won't wipe your programming.
  • Bypass included with every unit — it comes with the valve. Never order bypasses separately.
  • Programming is covered in Valve Programming. Here, just know it sits on top of the tank and controls flow and regeneration.

The media tank

The fiberglass tank that holds all the filtering and softening media.

  • NSF-certified fiberglass.
  • Standard builds ship on a 10"×54" or 12"×52" tank depending on configuration — check the work order. (The unit is still commonly called a "10×54.")

The media inside the tank

Three materials, layered — order matters. Tanks arrive pre-loaded from the factory; you never load media on site.

  • Carbon (coconut-shell activated carbon) sits on top. Water hits it first — it removes chlorine, chloramine, and chemicals, and protects the resin below. Activated grade on city water; catalytic grade on wells.
  • Softening resin (high-grade resin) sits below the carbon — the ion-exchange resin that removes hardness.
  • Gravel sits at the bottom — it supports the media bed and anchors the distributor tube. The gravel underbedding is included, and like every wetted component — valve, tank, carbon, resin, and gravel — it's NSF third-party certified.

On a single-tank city build, the mix is a half cubic foot of carbon over a full cubic foot of resin. That ratio is deliberate: on city water both medias wear out at roughly the same time, so nothing gets replaced early.

Why carbon goes above resin: chlorine breaks resin down fast. The carbon strips chlorine before the water ever touches the resin.

The distributor tube

The center tube that runs down the middle of the media tank.

  • The bottom looks like a pine cone with tiny holes, anchored by the gravel.
  • It's a 1.25" tube — part of how the system holds ~28 GPM with no pressure loss. Competitors run a 3/4" riser; the wider 1-1/4" tube is why flow rate isn't sacrificed.
  • This is the return path: treated water comes back up through it to head into the house.

The brine (salt) tank

A separate tank that holds the salt, sitting next to the media tank — connected to the valve, but not the same vessel.

  • The resin softens water by swapping hardness ions for sodium. When the resin is used up, the valve pulls brine from this tank to recharge it. That's regeneration.
  • Dimensions vary by build — confirm with the office if you need to verify a delivery.

The under-sink RO (separate system)

The drinking-water system at the kitchen sink. Its own install (RO Install), not part of the tank.

  • Two kinds: the tankless RO (HW800, 7-stage, compact, on-demand — needs electricity) and the tank RO (5-stage, small storage tank — works without electricity).
  • Alkaline remineralizer: tankless only. The tankless RO has it built in. The tank RO does not — it takes a separate external alkaline filter, which is leak-prone; that's why we prefer the built-in.
  • Tankless RO waste ratio: 2:1.

Add-ons you'll also see

Two add-ons show up on work orders — this is why the Before the Job kit list includes brass fittings.

  • UV purifier (wells): a stainless reactor on the main line that disinfects bacteria and viruses with UV light — no change to taste or odor. 120V (needs an outlet), 1" MNPT inlet/outlet.
  • Pre-system sediment filter (optional add-on): the carbon tank already acts as a natural pre-sediment filter for normal grit, so this is an add-on for homes with heavy sediment — a 5-micron, 20"×4.5" cartridge at the main entry that catches sand, silt, and rust before the tanks. Common on wells and older infrastructure; cartridge swaps roughly every 6 months.

How the water flows through it

Follow the water and the whole thing makes sense.

  • In: water enters the top of the media tank through the valve.
  • Down through carbon: chlorine and chemicals come out first, protecting the resin below.
  • Down through resin: ion exchange pulls out the hardness.
  • Through the gravel and up the center tube: water reaches the bottom, passes through the gravel, and travels back up the distributor tube.
  • Out to the house: soft, filtered water heads to every tap.
  • Drinking water: the kitchen tap gets a final polish at the separate under-sink RO.

Short version: in the top, down through carbon then resin, up the center tube, out to the house — drinking water finished at the RO.

The system types you'll encounter

Most installs are the standard city setup, but you'll see all of these.

Standard whole-home + RO (city)

The most common install by far.

  • One media tank (softener + filter combined), a brine tank, and the under-sink tankless RO for drinking water.
  • Rated 48,000 grains, roughly 28 GPM. The capacity you program at the valve is a lower regen setpoint — use Valve Programming's settings table, never the rated number.
  • Fits a standard city home — up to about 6 people, 4 bathrooms or fewer.

📹 Video: Deep dive on the standard whole-home unit and how it's built — watch it here

XL

The standard, sized up.

  • Rated 64,000 grains, on a 12"×52" media tank.
  • For larger homes — the standard unit covers 1–6 people, so step up to the XL (or a dual-tank) at 8+ residents or 4+ bathrooms (sqft is secondary).
  • Below that size, the standard unit is fine — the wider distributor tube keeps flow up, so there's no flow decrease staying on it.

Dual-Tank City

A premium two-tank version for city water.

  • Carbon and softening resin live in separate tanks — the carbon tank is a 10"×60".
  • Both tanks self-clean (backwash) off one smart valve — the carbon tank uses a simple pass-through / in-and-out valve, not a second expensive electronic one. Most competitors put a valve only on the resin tank and leave the carbon as a passive "passenger" that gets swapped every 2–3 years; because ours backwashes the carbon too, that tank lasts 10–20+ years.
  • Rated 96,000 grains total (48k resin + 48k carbon).
  • The resin never sees chlorinated water, so both medias last much longer than in a single tank. Expect roughly 12–20 years from a dual-tank, and 10–20+ years from its carbon tank — versus ~7–12 years from a single-tank build.

Well / Iron & Sulfur

Well water needs different media — and above 7 ppm iron, a different system entirely.

  • Dual-Tank Well: for wells. Adds Katalox Light media (removes iron, boosts pH, self-cleans) and catalytic coconut carbon, running an air injection valve. Handles iron up to 7 ppm.

  • Iron & Sulfur (H₂O₂): for wells with iron over 7 ppm or a sulfur / rotten-egg smell. Adds a hydrogen-peroxide injection panel and holding tank ahead of the tanks — the peroxide rusts the iron so the carbon can filter it out, and off-gasses the sulfur. Runs dual valves that stagger regeneration so one brine tank isn't asked to recharge both at once.

Salt-Free

A conditioner, not a softener — important distinction.

  • No brine tank, no salt, no drain, no valve, no electricity.
  • It conditions the water to prevent scale but does not remove hardness — 10 GPG in is 10 GPG out. It only reduces scale adhesion; it does not actually soften the water or give the silky feel a real softener does.
  • The media is TAC/NAC. You cannot mix carbon into the same tank — that makes the media fail — so a salt-free job needs a separate carbon tank (~$300 dealer) for chlorine/chemical removal.
  • Not recommended for well water.

Before the Job — Prep & What to Bring

Everything that happens before you cut a single pipe: how the equipment reaches you, what to carry, what every site needs, and the install types you'll run into. This section owns the packing list — when any other section mentions parts or tools, this is the list it means.

Who provides what

We hand you the equipment. You bring the trade skills, your own tools, and the raw install materials to tie it in.

We provide (per job):

  • The whole-home softener — control valve, media tank, brine tank.
  • The under-sink RO drinking system — filter bank and faucet.
  • The small parts — brine-tank clip, O-rings, basket, overflow fitting, and the flow restrictor gasket (comes in the white box).
  • One 40 lb bag of softener salt — pour it into the brine tank at handoff and leave the empty bag so the customer knows what to buy.

! The flow restrictor is a must-install, not a spare. The donut-shaped rubber gasket goes on the drain line and limits regeneration waste to 50–60 gallons — skip it and every regen wastes hundreds of gallons. The install step is in The Install SOP's drain phase.

You bring (your tools):

  • Sawzall — cuts the wall opening when there's a board behind the sheetrock.
  • Drywall saw — for a clean sheetrock-only cut.
  • Milwaukee reamer — deburrs cut pipe ends.
  • Crimper — for the fittings.
  • Diamond drill bit — 1/2" or 5/8", either works — for drilling the countertop (granite/quartz) for the RO faucet hole. Straight from the manufacturer's training: "a half-inch or a five-eighths diamond drill bit is what you need for drilling through countertops for the faucet."

You supply (install materials):

  • Pipe — including curved "road" pipe for gentle bends off the valve.
  • 3/8" lines.
  • CPVC for the drain run (rated for pressure and hot water).
  • Fittings, 90s, and rings.
  • Thread tape (Blue Monster) and pipe dope.
  • Brass fittings if the work order includes a UV purifier or pre-sediment filter (see the add-ons in The Systems).

Sizes and quantities: confirm your standard kit with the office on your first pickup — don't guess at diameters or counts.

Brass fittings — the per-install count: every install takes two 1" female fittings (one per tail) and one 3/4" female (the drain) — "two tails, one drain." Buy them by the case at a plumbing supply house like Ferguson — roughly $12 a fitting, about $200 a case — instead of one-offs at a big-box store. Most homes you'll tie into are running PEX.

Where the small parts ride: the clips, O-rings, basket, and overflow fitting ship inside the brine tank. The kit includes a spare set of the small O-rings — but there is only ONE big O-ring, no spare. Do not lose it. Everything in that tank is required — if any of it is missing, you cannot complete the install, so check it at pickup, not at the customer's house.

Test kit: final verification (Startup & Testing) needs a hardness drop test, a chlorine test, and a TDS meter. The office supplies your test kit — you don't source your own. It's the standard WET International hardness + chlorine + iron kit (~$100–110; the TDS meter is a cheap separate buy). If you don't have one before your first job, ask the office to send it.

Getting the equipment

  • Systems and equipment are picked up at our warehouse — the location is on the work order.
  • If anything ships freight instead: inspect the pallet, photograph all sides, and document any damage BEFORE anyone signs — you or the homeowner. A signed-for damaged tank is an unclaimable damaged tank.

The work order also carries the system configuration and the home's tested hardness (GPG). If either is missing, call the office before job day.

Verify before you leave the truck

Confirm you have every part and every tool before you start — check it yourself, don't assume the warehouse packed it right. Finishing details eat the most time on a job, so keep extras of everything on the truck. A missing clip or fitting kills an install mid-job.

The 3 things every install needs

A site is installable when all three are accessible. If any one is missing or unreachable, the job changes — or it's not a job yet.

  • Main water line — to tie the system into.
  • Drain — to carry water out during regeneration.
  • Power source — a standard outlet. The unit runs on a standard 110/120V plug — no hardwiring — and the power cord is about 10 feet long. If no outlet is within reach, you have options: splice/extend the cord, run an extension cord, fish the line through the wall behind an existing outlet's face plate, or sub a cheap electrician (~$150) to add an outlet.

! Septic (well/country homes): never route the regen drain into a septic tank — the salty regen water kills the tank's bacteria. Run it outside instead, about 3 ft underground in a flowerbed. If a septic customer asks about drain routing beyond that, call the office — don't freelance local discharge rules.

If all three are accessible, it's installable. The install type comes down to where the main line is exposed.

Locating the main line and shut-off valve

Where the shut-off valve sits determines the install type, so pin it down first — ideally before you're on site.

The main is only ever in about three places:

  • Garage / indoor — on the wall, by the softener loop, or behind an access panel.
  • Flowerbed / yard — in an in-ground box.
  • City / street — in the meter box at the curb.

Softener loop vs. no loop

  • Loop present — a garage loop is the cleanest tie-in. Treat it as the exception, not the default.
  • No loop — most jobs. You're tying into a bare shut-off valve, often in the garage. Plan the tie-in and drain run yourself.

  • Bury — the valve and fiberglass tank are fine outside with no cover needed; the faux rock protects from the elements. Keep the valve exposed for service.
  • Hard freeze (outdoor units) — tell the customer to drip a faucet until it passes.
  • Outdoor, trenching, and bury installs carry extra fees and need photos collected up front so the job is quoted right.

The Install — Step-by-Step (Field SOP)

By the end of this section the system is fully plumbed and mounted, the bypass is in BYPASS, and the main water is back on. Programming (Valve Programming) and startup (Startup & Testing) come after — nothing regenerates in this section.

Before You Touch a Wrench: The Golden Rule

Everything downstream depends on where you tie into the main. Get this wrong and the job fails no matter how clean the rest of your work is.

🥇 GOLDEN RULE — Tie into the main BEFORE the line tees off to the water heater.

  • Tie in before the water-heater tee → you treat both hot and cold.
  • Tie in after the tee → you treat cold only — the water heater keeps getting hard water.

Find the tee before you cut anything, and make sure your tie-in sits upstream of it.

The Water Sequence — Off, Bypass, Back On

The whole section runs on one water sequence. Know it before Phase 1:

  • Stage everything BEFORE the water goes off. Make your cuts on the bench, run the drain, prep every fitting — then shut the main off as late as possible. The customer stays in water while you work; the goal is minutes without water, not an hour.
  • The main goes OFF just before you cut into the live line — you locate the shutoff in Phase 1.
  • Open a faucet and leave it open the whole time the water is off. That's your air escape path.
  • The unit is plumbed in with the bypass valve in BYPASS.
  • Once your tie-in is complete and the joints are checked, the main goes back ON — in bypass, the house gets full city water at full pressure, just unsoftened, so the customer isn't without water while you finish.
  • Push all the air out as pressure returns — like bleeding brakes on a car. Air trapped in the line has to come out somewhere, and if you leave it in there, "somewhere" can be a leak at any joint you plumbed, even inside the wall. Let the open faucet spit and sputter until it runs smooth.
  • Eyeball the tie-in joints as pressure returns — re-checking your own joints is what keeps you off return calls.
  • The system stays in bypass until Startup & Testing startup. The bypass is fully open or fully closed, never in between — it controls pressure, not softness. The one deliberate exception is first-regen startup, where you crack it partway on purpose — see Startup & Testing.
  • Never run a cycle in this section. The first regeneration happens in Startup & Testing, after the valve is programmed in Valve Programming.

The Tanks Arrive Ready

  • Every tank is assembled and media-loaded at our factory in Fort Worth. There is no on-site media loading — ever. If a tank arrives looking unloaded or damaged, stop and call the office.
  • Whether the brine tank takes an initial water fill before the first cycle isn't covered here — confirm with the office before your first job.

Phase 1 — Job Setup & Wall Assessment

Set yourself up so nothing stops the job halfway through, and learn the wall before you cut it.

Confirm parts and tools first

  • Check that you personally have every part and tool before starting.
  • Don't trust the warehouse pull — verify it yourself.

Find the main and read the pipe

  • Locate the main water shutoff, typically behind the access panel — this is what you close before cutting in.
  • Check the pipe size so you know what you're tying into.
  • Plumbing type is usually PEX. If the main is copper or galvanized, call the office before you cut — the tie-in method below assumes PEX.
  • Clear the work area and set up a flat staging surface in the garage.

Reach into the wall by hand

Before any cutting, reach into the wall cavity by hand and confirm three things:

  • Where the line tees off to the water heater — so you honor the Golden Rule.
  • The wall material — a board behind the sheetrock means you'll need a Sawzall, not just a drywall saw.
  • The plumbing type.

Line location, tee location, and wall material — all known before you make a single hole. No surprises mid-cut.

Phase 2 — Plan the Drain

Decide where the softener drains and how you'll run the pipe before you start fitting anything.

Where to tie the drain

  • Tie into the water heater's TPR (temperature/pressure relief) drain — that's our standard.
  • If your local code doesn't allow tying into the TPR discharge, stop and call the office before improvising.
  • Know the pressure difference: the TPR drain is pressurized; the softener system is not (it runs on its own pressure only).
  • Use CPVC for the drain run — it's rated for both pressure and hot water.

Make it a real, secured connection

  • The regen drain must be a formal, secured plumbing connection — not a loose hose. During regeneration the drain line pushes ~50 gallons/hour with real pressure, and an unsecured hose will blow out.
  • Never shove a drain hose into a vent stack. One installer forced a drain line through an attic vent stack and caulked it — the pressure pushed it right back out and flooded roughly $40k of damage.

Fitting philosophy

  • Use the fewest fittings possible — every fitting is a potential leak point.
  • Favor vertical over horizontal. If a fitting ages out, gravity helps you rather than working against you. Horizontal is acceptable, not ideal.

Phase 3 — Assemble the Control Head & Bypass

Build up the control head with the small parts that cause guaranteed callbacks if they're missed.

The components

  • Bypass valve, control valve, head, and tails.

The two parts you cannot skip

  • Seat the bottom O-ring — mandatory. Miss it and it leaks 100% of the time.
  • Install the brine-tank clip that secures the tube — miss it and the line leaks and pops off, which means a callback.

Keep the clip and other small parts in a side pocket so they don't get lost on the job.

Basket and fittings

  • Install the basket: line up the four grooves until it clicks.
  • Seat every fitting fully — lube if you have it, push down until you hear it pop, then turn it with real muscle. Don't be scared to lean into it. If it comes off a little, pop it back on — and make sure it's tight, because a loose one will leak through.
  • Verify tightness and re-seat anything that feels loose.

Phase 4 — Flow Meter & Flow Components

Set the flow meter and flow components, and check that nothing is pinched before you move on.

  • Install the flow meter and tighten it down.
  • Inspect every ring and fitting — nothing pinched, popping out, or misaligned. A pinched ring will work its way out and leak.
  • Flow meter orientation: long side IN, and the arrows point IN toward the inlet.
  • If it's unclear which line the flow meter goes on for your build, call the office — don't guess.

Phase 5 — Drain Threading & Flow Restrictor

Seal the drain threads and install the one part that saves hundreds of gallons per regen.

Thread sealing

  • Apply thread tape plus pipe dope to the drain threads. Not strictly required since the drain isn't pressurized — but better safe.
  • Blue Monster tape technique: wrap the threads, then run your fingers over them and twirl. When the lines darken, the tape has caught.

Install the flow restrictor — non-negotiable

  • The flow restrictor is the color-coded rubber donut/gasket that ships inside the white valve box — it's not bought separately. It installs on the drain line.
  • It limits regeneration discharge to 50–60 gallons per cycle. Skip it and every regen wastes hundreds of gallons.
  • The correct color for the unit size and how the donut seats are specified in the paper manual inside that white box. Match the color to your unit and follow the manual for orientation — don't guess.

Phase 6 — Mount the Wall Plate & Cut the Hole

Use the plate's own guide to mark the opening, then cut with the right tool for the wall.

  • Use the wall plate's built-in measuring guide — fold it in and hold it in place.
  • Mark the four corners only. Don't draw a full outline.
  • Cut the opening. Reach for the Sawzall if you felt a board back in Phase 1.

Phase 7 — Route the Softener Drain Line

Run the softener drain out of the house the same way the water heater drains.

  • Run the softener drain along the wall behind the water heater and out, draining back to the city — just like the heater does.
  • ! Septic home? Never route the regen drain into the septic tank — see the septic rule in Before the Job (run it outside, ~3 ft underground in a flowerbed).
  • Minimize fittings.
  • Finish the pipe end with a 90 + ring every time. You'll need a 90 regardless of which direction you're heading.

Phase 8 — Shape & Fit the Pipe

Shape the pipe to fit without disturbing the ring that holds everything in place.

! Watch the ring

  • Fittings rubbing against the ring push the clip out. Don't force a fitting against it.

Shaping the pipe

  • Use curved "road" pipe — add only a gentle curve, never a hard bend.
  • Ream the cut ends with a Milwaukee reamer for clean, burr-free holes.

Phase 9 — Crimp & Mount the Plate

Crimp the fittings and screw the plate down so it's solid.

Crimping

  • Crimp the fittings, keeping them turned to the side — they rotate, which gives you room for the initial crimp.
  • Pre-position the fittings before you measure the plate dots.
  • Ring sizes and crimp-tool spec for our standard build: confirm with the office before your first job.

Mounting the plate

  • Mount the plate with screws — more secure than the clips alone.

Phase 10 — Brine Tank Assembly

Build the brine tank and lock it down. Getting the air out and confirming the connection here is what keeps water off the floor.

Bleed and seat

Before You Start

The valve does the work automatically once it's set. Your job here is to enter the right numbers.

Three things to know going in:

  • The valve runs demand regeneration — programmed with the home's hardness, it counts gallons and regenerates based on actual usage, not a fixed schedule. The algorithm turns the programmed hardness into a gallon counter and ticks it down as water is used; when the counter hits zero, it regenerates.
  • You set the regeneration time to 2:00 a.m. and a max-days backstop so it self-cleans even in a low-usage home. If the gallon counter hasn't reached zero within the day window, the valve force-regenerates anyway — it has to clean itself at least once a month. Net effect: roughly one regen a month, at 2 a.m., in normal use.
  • The single most important number is water hardness, and it comes off the work order — the office tested the home's water and put the GPG on your paperwork. Get it wrong and you get hard-water breakthrough and a callback. Programming these numbers wrong has caused system failures and tank swaps — take the time to enter them correctly.

Under-Sink RO Install

This section covers the under-sink reverse-osmosis drinking system — the faucet, the unit, the storage, and the leak check — so you install ours the same way every time and never leave a slow leak behind a cabinet.

What You're Installing

The RO is a separate drinking-water system that lives under the kitchen sink and feeds a dedicated faucet on the counter. It is independent of the whole-home softener — its own supply tap, its own filter bank, its own drain connection.

Two versions ship, depending on the job:

  • HW800 AlkaPro tankless RO — the standard on most city bundles. 7-stage, 800 GPD, on-demand (no storage tank), with a built-in alkaline remineralizer and auto-flush. Finished water comes out around 8.5–9.0 pH. Needs electricity.
  • Tank RO — a steel-shell storage tank with a food-grade butyl bladder that pressurizes RO water for steady faucet flow. Works without electricity. No built-in alkaline stage — remineralizing takes a separate external add-on filter.

Both strip contaminants down through the membrane; only the tankless unit adds healthy minerals back natively (on a tank RO that's an external add-on filter). The tankless unit filters down to 0.0001µm, and its NSF thin-film RO membrane runs 95–99% rejection on TDS, heavy metals, microplastics, PFAs, and bacteria/viruses.

Filter Stages

The tankless unit is multi-stage internally — that's the "7-stage" on the label. Water runs through a pre-carbon block, a sediment filter, the RO membrane, a post-carbon block, and an alkaline filter (plus a few additional stages), and the alkaline filter is now built in by the manufacturer rather than added on. The "7-stage" count and the "three filters" you see are not a contradiction: only three of those cartridges are user-replaceable, and those are the three that sit behind the removable magnetic faceplate. Water moves through the replaceable three in order:

  • Pre-carbon — the large fridge-style filter; first-stage carbon.
  • RO membrane — the fine membrane that removes everything.
  • Post-carbon with built-in alkaline remineralizer — final polish that adds minerals back.

A tank RO has no built-in alkaline stage — it takes an external add-on filter instead, and those external filters are a known leak point. Know which unit you're on before you quote a filter change.

Yearly filter change (the pop-out cartridges): turn off the angle-stop where the RO ties into the cold-water line, open the RO faucet to relieve the pressure, then push the cartridge button and the filter pops out.

Plan the Sink First

Before you drill anything, assess the sink and plan the whole layout.

  • Tankless RO: confirm a usable outlet in the sink cabinet before you start. The HW800 has an electric pump and needs a 110V outlet under the kitchen sink. Never show up with a tankless if there's no under-sink outlet — carry a tank-style RO as backup. Watch for the outlet being switched off the garbage-disposal switch — check that it's actually live before you commit. No outlet → call the office before starting — don't drill a countertop for a unit you can't power.
  • Confirm whether the sink already has a spare hole for the drinking faucet. A new dedicated faucet hole often needs to be drilled.
  • Check the countertop material. Granite and quartz are harder to drill — that's what the 1/2"–5/8" diamond bit in your kit (Before the Job) is for.
  • Plan the layout for symmetry — the RO unit on one side of the cabinet, the faucet run on the other.
  • Map where the tubing and any wiring will run behind the fixtures so no line ends up under tension. Lines under constant tension leak.

Drill the Faucet Hole

Drill the dedicated faucet hole through the countertop.

  • Drill the new hole on the right side of the sink (or mirror it) so the faucet sits symmetric and the unit can live on the opposite side of the cabinet.
  • Technique on granite/quartz: start the diamond bit at a 45° notch, then bring it straight, and add water while drilling to cool the bit. The diamond bit won't crack the stone, so don't panic about that — the only realistic downside is a surface scratch, about a $200 buff/repolish. Nicks are rare (~$200–250 to polish out if it happens) — take your time.
  • Catch the stone dust — never let it wash down the garbage disposal. Catch it by hand and wipe up with wet napkins.
  • Once the hole is through, the faucet supply line pokes up about ¼" above the countertop so the faucet seats cleanly.
  • The hole is small — the faucet's cover disc (escutcheon) fully covers it, so there's no visible gap.

Mount the Faucet

The drinking faucet mounts through the fresh hole and seals from both sides.

  • Install it with both rubber gaskets. The cover disc is larger than the gasket rings, so there's no leak path around it.
  • The spout swivels; the base stays fixed. It hand-tightens clockwise — no wrench needed.
  • Seat it snug so it doesn't spin loose, but this is a hand-tight fitting.

Run the Tubing

Route every line behind the fixtures and leave yourself room to service the unit later.

  • Keep all tubing behind the fixtures, never pulled tight. A line under tension is a future leak.
  • Leave slack on every line — the most slack on the faucet line.
  • Never cut the faucet line short. The slack is what lets you pull the whole unit out of the cabinet for a filter change or service.

Bring It Into Service — the First Regeneration

Do not put the system into service straight off the truck. The first thing the softener does on your install is a full manual regeneration/backwash.

Why it's mandatory

The first regen rinses the carbon media's fines and dust out to drain and settles the media in the tank. Skip it and the carbon underperforms from day one because the fines never rinse out.

Run it

  • Kick off the regeneration. The brine tank isn't needed during backwash — but it must be connected before the countdown hits zero, or water shoots out the open port.
  • The first backwash runs about 6 minutes — experienced installers use that window to finish the brine tank.
  • Bypass technique: crack the bypass open slowly — halfway during the FIRST backwash, then all the way open during the SECOND backwash. Once fully open it stays fully open — never in between.
  • After the first backwash, skip straight to the second backwash (don't sit through the intermediate cycles).
  • Let the cycles run fully to completion, and stay with the unit — this is your leak check under real flow.
  • Don't pre-fill the brine tank with water. The system fills it on its own — water shows up in the tank roughly 30 minutes in, about when you're wrapping up. Just start it with the one bag of salt.

Post-Install Water Tests

Now prove the system delivers. The rule that trips up new installers is what you test where — get it wrong and a working system looks like a failed install.

Test kit: hardness vial + Solutions 1 & 2, chlorine vial + reagent, TDS meter. The kit is ordered from WET International (the "Honest Water" hardness + chlorine kit, plus iron — see Before the Job). Confirm with the office whether you or they place the order.

The one rule to memorize

  • Whole-home (softened) water: test hardness (chlorine optional). Never test TDS here.
  • RO faucet water: test TDS only.

A softener does not lower TDS, so a TDS reading on whole-home water barely moves after install — it looks broken even though it's working perfectly. TDS only drops on water that has passed through the RO.

Hardness test — cold supply

Test the cold supply — the hot side can carry residual hardness from the old water heater.

Tools: hardness vial, Solution 1 (indicator), Solution 2 (titrant), cold faucet water.

  • Fill the vial with cold water to the "1 grain per gallon" line.
  • Add 7 drops of Solution 1, cap, and shake.
  • Add Solution 2 one drop at a time, counting, until the color turns.

How to read it:

  • Turns blue immediately, never pink = 0 GPG. That's your pass.
  • If it goes pink first, the number of Solution 2 drops to turn it blue = grains per gallon. You want 0.

Two things so a good install doesn't read as a failure:

  • Old water heaters may briefly show ~2 GPG on the hot side — that's residual, not a bad install. Test the cold supply.
  • A full hardness retest at the customer's faucet is best done about a month out — residual hardness in the supply lines and water heater needs time to flush, or the cold side can still read high right after install.

Chlorine test (optional)

Optional at post-install, but useful to confirm the carbon is working.

Tools: chlorine vial, chlorine reagent, faucet water.

  • Fill the vial to the 10 ml line.
  • Add 5–10 chlorine drops and watch the color develop (yellow → dark yellow → brown; darker = more chlorine).

Post-carbon, you want a noticeably lighter result than the pre-install reading.

TDS test — RO faucet only

This proves the RO drinking system. Test the RO faucet, not the sink faucet.

Tools: TDS meter, cup.

  • Fill a cup from the RO faucet.
  • Power on the meter, dip it, and wait for a stable reading.

How to read it:

  • RO water should read ~100 ppm or less (how low depends on the source water).
  • The RO retest can be done right after install — no waiting period.
  • Never run TDS on the whole-home softened water — the number barely changes and looks like a failed install.

Troubleshooting & Callbacks

Most callbacks aren't broken systems — they're normal break-in behavior. Learn the field tells, prevent the avoidable callbacks on install day, and know the short list of situations that are genuinely a warranty claim.

The mindset: lead with reassurance, not a truck

A freshly installed system needs time to clear old water out of the home's pipes, and some first-week behavior (white particles from the RO's remineralization, residual hardness working out) is completely normal. Before you schedule a return trip, work the symptom against the causes below — a short, confident explanation on the phone solves most first-week calls.

  • Find the symptom, then check causes in order.
  • Lead with the reassurance line before rolling a truck.
  • If it's genuinely a fault, see "When it IS a warranty claim."

Softener symptoms

"The water isn't soft" (just installed)

Work these in order — most land on cause 1 or 2.

  • Just installed. The home takes about 30 minutes to come into soft water as residual hard water clears the pipes. Longer in two-story homes.
  • Hot water still a little hard. Normal for a while. An old water heater is full of buildup, and soft water leaches it back out — expect roughly 2 GPG on the hot side, temporarily. Test the cold supply, not the hot: titration drops that turn blue immediately mean 0 GPG, which means the system is working.
  • Out of salt. An empty brine tank means no regeneration, which means hard water. Refill the salt and run a regeneration. Resin does not just die — if it tested good past about 60 days, the failure is a lack of salt, not the equipment.
  • Hardness setting too low. Confirm the valve is programmed to the tested GPG on the work order — the factory default setting (10) under-treats most local water, and a too-low setting lets hardness break through between regenerations. Reprogram if needed (Valve Programming).
  • Genuinely dead within weeks. Rare — this is a warranty path. See "When it IS a warranty claim."

"It regenerates every night"

  • The schedule got thrown off. A power outage can reset the days-to-regen counter to zero, so it cycles nightly. Reprogram it (Valve Programming).
  • An undersized old unit can also over-regenerate — relevant if you're servicing equipment that isn't ours.

"The water stopped softening" (tank may be failing)

A failing tank shows up as a slight water-pressure dip plus noticeably worse water quality — the resin "goops up" inside. When you see that, put the system on bypass at the valve so the home runs on raw city water for now, then call the office to schedule a tank swap.

"There's dirty water in the salt tank"

  • Normal. That's the brine solution sitting in the tank, not the home's drinking water. It's supposed to sit there, and it can look murky.

"It makes noise at night"

  • Normal. That's the regeneration and backwash cycle — think of it as a salt-water car wash. The valve regenerates on demand, roughly once a month in normal use, typically around 2 a.m.

"Will the drain line freeze?" (Iowa/Illinois winters)

  • No. The drain line only carries water during regeneration — roughly once a month (the valve regenerates on demand) — so it sits empty almost all the time and is never stagnant.
  • On a hard-freeze night with an outdoor install, tell the customer to drip a faucet as a precaution.

"The brine tank is overflowing"

  • Usually too much salt — four bags can push water out the overflow. Recommend keeping it around three bags.
  • If salt level is fine, the overflow fitting may simply be doing its job.

RO symptoms

"The RO stopped making water"

  • Reset it first. Unplug the unit for 10 seconds, then plug it back in. The built-in safety auto-resets.
  • Lights flashing red means a filter is due. Change all filters together — the others are only days behind. Standard service is all three filters every 12 months at the annual visit.
  • No power. A tankless RO needs electricity — check the outlet and the GFCI. (A tank RO still works without power.)

"The RO is leaking"

  • Customer stopgap over the phone: have them turn the shutoff T sideways to kill water to the RO only. The rest of the house is unaffected. Then schedule the visit.

  • On the bench, the usual leak points are:

  • lines under tension

  • lines cut too short
  • external add-on alkaline filters (this is why we prefer the built-in remineralization)

"The fridge ice/water has been weird since the install"

  • This is why we talk customers out of fridge tie-ins — pressure and communication issues make the outcome roughly a coin flip, and we only do one when the office has booked it.
  • If a fridge was tied in, quirks like this are expected — offer to disconnect it if it keeps misbehaving.

Install-day gotchas (prevent the callback)

Most callbacks are prevented on the truck, not fixed later. Build these into your routine on every job.

  • Tie in before the water-heater tee. Tie in after it and you only treat the cold water — the water heater keeps getting hard water. Hand-check the wall cavity first to confirm where you are in the run.
  • Never run a regen with the brine tank disconnected — water shoots out of the open port.
  • Power the RO on while it's still out in the open and leak-check it before tucking it back under the sink.

Pre-leak checklist (softener)

Run this before you call the job done — any one of these missed becomes a callback.

  • Seat the bottom O-ring. Missing it is a 100% leak.
  • Install the brine-tank clip. Missing it lets the line pop off.
  • No pinched rings.
  • Flow restrictor on the drain. Skip it and every regeneration wastes hundreds of gallons.

Well and site conditions

  • Well dual-tank: the Katalox tank takes the downflow / air-injection valve. Verify the labeled valve is on the correct tank before you install. Not 100% sure which valve goes on which tank? Call the office — don't guess.
  • Septic homes: never route the regen drain into the septic tank — the salty regen water kills the tank's bacteria. Run it outside, about 3 feet underground in a flowerbed.
  • Galvanized pipes (older Iowa/Illinois homes): soft water leaches metal off galvanized pipe. Flag it to the customer and recommend a repipe where feasible.
  • High incoming pressure (around 120 PSI): the home needs a pressure regulator. It's not our system's issue, but flag it for the customer.

Bring the right parts

  • Bring the right faucet color and the right RO type for the job. Showing up with the wrong one is the top cause of return trips.

Field tells: fast water-quality diagnosis

A quick look around the home tells you what you're dealing with before you test anything.

First-weeks "is this normal?" calls

These are the calls that come in during the first days and weeks. Almost all are normal break-in behavior. Know the reassurance line so you can settle the customer on the spot — or walk them through the quick fix.

White particles / foggy water from the new RO faucet

  • Normal — do not roll a truck. The tankless RO remineralizes — it adds calcium and magnesium back into the water, and that's what the customer is seeing.
  • What to tell them: "That's expected and completely harmless — your new RO puts healthy minerals back into the water, and that's what you're seeing. It clears up on its own."

"The water doesn't feel slimy/soft yet"

  • Walk them through the fix: have them hold down the regen button to force a regeneration, and confirm there's salt in the brine tank.
  • What to tell them: "No system makes water any slimier than another — what you're feeling is old hardness still sitting in your pipes getting picked up as it clears out. A quick hardness test will almost certainly show your water is fully soft."
  • If still in doubt, offer a hardness test on the cold supply — drops turn blue immediately means 0 GPG, which means it's working.

"The water is coming out yellow since the install"

  • Walk them through the fix: turn on about five faucets and run them simultaneously for roughly 10 minutes straight to clear it.
  • Cause: the new soft water grabs leftover hardness inside the pipes, plus a little new resin/carbon bleeding off into the water at first.
  • What to tell them: "It's not dangerous — it just looks ugly. Run several faucets together for about ten minutes and it'll clear right up."

"Will the softener hurt my lawn / sprinklers?"

  • What to tell them: "The system doesn't touch your irrigation or sprinkler system — it only treats the water inside the house plus your hose bibs. That's how about 99% of homes are plumbed, so your lawn gets the same water it always has."

"Could the softener discharge corrode my septic?"

  • Acknowledge it's a valid concern, then reassure. On septic homes we don't route the regen drain into the septic tank at all — salty regen water is bad for the tank's bacteria, so the drain runs outside instead.
  • It's a small, infrequent discharge anyway — about 50–60 gallons at around 2 a.m., roughly once a month.
  • If the customer asks to move or re-route the drain line: don't improvise a new location on the spot. Local discharge rules vary — confirm with the office before promising anything.

When it IS a warranty claim

Most calls are not warranty claims. The short list that qualifies:

  • Dead or damaged out of the box, or failing within weeks. Document it with photo or video proof for the replacement. Expect this on roughly 1 in 50 jobs.
  • Freight damage. This must be documented before signing for the pallet (photos, all sides — see Before the Job).

Anything outside that list — no salt, wrong settings, normal break-in behavior — is not a warranty item.

Tank swap

When a tank has to be replaced, reuse the existing valve and just swap the tank — about a 10-minute job. Always install a fresh tank; don't vacuum out the old media and refill it. Dealer cost is roughly $300 for a carbon tank and $400 for a resin tank.
